### Cleaning Machine Function and Introduction, Pros and Cons (Who Should Buy a Cleaning Machine)

It is not surprising that Bissell invented the cleaning machine because it has long been renowned as a leading brand in cleaning appliances in America. The water-based cleaning products category, particularly, is where Bissell dominates North America. Initially, the cleaning machine started with carpet washers and then adapted to velvety brushes for hard floors based on Chinese market characteristics.

(Carpet Washer — Cleaning Machine Image)

Currently, the advantages of cleaning machines are as follows:

- All dry and wet waste can be handled; the mopping module is suitable for the Chinese market.
- The self-cleaning function simplifies subsequent brush cleaning.

However, cleaning machines do not solve all problems as some influencers claim. Currently, there are several issues:

1. Water Stains: Due to continuous water injection into the brushes by the pump, this achieves real-time live water cleaning of the brushes. However, the brushes remain wet, making the floor continuously damp (imagine using a wet towel to mop). During our live stream, many friends purchased various brands of cleaning machines and returned some due to water stains.

Solution: Most brands have similar water stain issues; dual-brush models tend to have more because they have a larger contact area.

2. Wet Brushes, Odor: Currently, the brush material is velvety fabric. After mopping and self-cleaning, the brushes are relatively moist. In some regions, during rainy seasons like plum rains, if the brushes do not dry easily, odors can develop. This is one of the most common complaints.

Solution: The industry mainly uses two solutions: 1) High-speed rotation for centrifugal drying; motors rotate actively to use scrapers to remove moisture. 2) Low-temperature low-speed drying; through separate drying speeds and low-speed warm air blowing to dry the brushes.

Both methods have similar effects, offering better performance than no drying at all. Some brands like Mifubao do not use velvety brushes, thus avoiding odor issues.

3. Dual vs Single Brush Selection?

Some readers might think that dual-brush models offer stronger cleaning power. However, in our tests, the cleaning power of dual-brush models was not superior to single-brush models. In actual use, single-brush models perform better overall. The advantage of dual brushes is their relative rotation; thus, dual-brush machines are easier to pull due to this assistance.

4. How to Choose a Cleaning Machine Motor?

Currently, most cleaning machine motors range from 250W to 350W. For hard floors, the usage environment is simple, so a brushless motor with about 250W of power can balance suction and battery life. Larger motor power requires larger batteries, leading to significant cost increases and weight gains.

For models like Dreame M12 or M13 that can be converted into vacuum cleaners when removing the dust bucket component, they use a 350W brushless motor for suction adjustment. However, note that even with a 350W motor, daily cleaning power is around 250W.

Additionally, most cleaning machine manufacturers use motors from the same supplier, so there are minimal differences. Therefore, you don't need to worry too much; a 250W brushless motor will suffice.

Moreover: Some cheaper models like Midea X7 or Deerma VX100 use ordinary brushed motors for cost reasons (about 2-3 times more expensive and significantly different in performance). The difference between brushed and brushless motors can be seen by choosing the latter. A simple method is that brushed motor speeds generally do not exceed 50,000 RPM.

6. **Are Electrolyzed Water or Disinfectant Solutions Useful?**

Since Tineco first introduced the concept of electrolyzed water/disinfectant solutions, converting tap water into hypochlorous acid for disinfection purposes, this feature is now a standard in high-end models. Many people also ask me whether this function is just a form of "tax on intelligence" or mere showmanship. First, it does have some effect but is limited.

Mainly two aspects:

1. Preparing electrolyzed water requires time; often, by the time you use it, the electrolysis might not be fully completed.
2. The disinfection effect of electrolyzed water diminishes over distance from the clean water tank to the roller brush.
3. Electrolyzed water is effective against certain bacteria but less so for others.

**Summary: Electrolyzed water/disinfectant solutions have some efficacy, albeit limited. This function does not need much consideration; the actual high-efficiency disinfection comes from the disinfectant solutions provided by various brands. These chemical disinfectants have been in use in the U.S. for decades and are both safe and effective.**

7. **Choosing Between Single-Edge and Double-Edge Mops**

Firstly, it is clear that a single-edge mop is better than no edge at all, and a double-edge mop is even better than a single-edge one. However, the difference in performance is minimal.

Primarily, edges are effective for cleaning corners; if there is some dry debris near the wall, any edge or non-edge model can clean it up. During mopping, machines without an edge might leave small areas uncleaned.

Currently, most mid-to-high-end models have started to include edges as standard features with no additional cost.

8. **Can Hard Floor Washers Use 84 Disinfectant or Dish Soap?**

Many people ask if they can use household dish soap or 84 disinfectant instead of the expensive cleaning solutions for their hard floor washers, which cost over $3000. According to our tests, these substances cannot be directly added to the hard floor washer as they have different pH levels that may damage plastic components inside.

Even between different brands, cleaning solutions are not interchangeable due to varying pH levels.

**Summary: Cleaning solutions cannot currently be used at will; costs might decrease significantly when the industry matures.**

We designed 17 tests based on the functions of hard floor washers and common issues encountered. The scoring principle is balanced, with no single test carrying too much weight, ensuring a fair assessment of both practical use and quality. Below are the details of our 17 testing methods and scores:

1. Dry Waste Test. We used cereal, sunflower shells, and yellow beans to simulate potential dry waste in homes, assessing each brand's handling capability for this type of waste. Most dry waste items, except for yellow beans, were easily managed. This test is relatively simple, with a total score of 7 points; one point is deducted for each unprocessed item.

(Testing photos)

2. Wet Waste Test. We used eggs, instant noodles, and a mixture of water and cereal to simulate potential wet waste in homes, evaluating the handling capability of various brands' hard floor washers. Currently, all tested brands performed well with wet waste management. The total score is 7 points.

(Testing photos)

3. Residual Waste Test in Suction Nozzles and Pipelines. After cleaning dry and wet waste, we checked for any residual waste in the brushes and pipelines. This test has a total score of 6 points.

(Testing photos)

4. Hair Test. We used both long and short hair to simulate home usage scenarios, primarily assessing whether hair gets tangled on the brush or clogs the air intake. The total score is 7 points.

(Testing photos)

5. Edge Cleaning Test. We mixed black rice with wastewater and placed it along the walls, dragging back and forth twice to see if there was any residue left behind. This test has a total score of 5 points.

(Testing photos)

6. Carpet Test. Although carpets are not used frequently, hard floor washers can still effectively clean them. We placed cereal on the carpet and dragged it several times to check for any residual waste. The total score is 5 points.

(Testing photos)

7. Front-Back Assistance. This test has a total score of 4 points, with 2 points each for front and back assistance. Single-brush models receive 2 points, while dual-brush models and those like Tineco 3.0 with rear assistance get 4 points.

(Testing photos)

8. Water Stain Test. Many readers are concerned about water stains after mopping. We poured 150ml of water on a mirror surface, dragged it back and forth twice to see if any water stains remain. This test scores 4 points. Currently, all tested models perform similarly.

(Testing photos)

9. Noise Test. The noise level for hard floor washers is generally between 70-80dBA; this test has a total score of 3 points, with all models scoring 3 points as long as the noise does not exceed 80dBa.

(Testing photos)

10. Stubborn Stain Test. The ability to handle stubborn stains is an advantage for hard floor washers, easily removing them through high-speed rotation. We dried old soy sauce with a hairdryer and dragged it three times with the washer to check for any residue. This test has a total score of 7 points.

(Testing photos)

11. Sewage Tank False Alarm Test. Some models' sewage tanks are prone to false alarms; we tested this by continuously suctioning until the machine alarmed and stopped. The total score is 4 points.

(Testing photos)

12. Brush Self-Cleaning Function. The self-cleaning function of hard floor washers is a core feature, with us submerging the brush in soy sauce for three rounds of cleaning to observe the effect after each round and check for any residual soy sauce. This test has a total score of 6 points.

(Testing photos)

13. Drying Test. If brushes are not dried properly, they may develop an odor; currently, washers mainly fall into categories without drying, hot air drying, or spin-drying.

14. Battery Life. We ran the machine continuously to check its battery life; models with a runtime of 40 minutes or more receive full marks. This test has a total score of 3 points.

15. Solid-Liquid Separation in Sewage Tank. Solid-liquid separation in the sewage tank aids subsequent processing, and this test has a total score of 5 points.

16. Bed Bottom Test. This test primarily evaluates the ability of hard floor washers to clean under beds; some models can only reach about 30cm below the bed, while others can fully access it. The total score is 5 points, with full marks for complete access and 3 points for partial access.

17. Quality Score. A quality score of 15 points is given without deductions if no issues are found during testing.

18. Bonus Points. We included as many features of mainstream hard floor washers in our tests as possible; however, some innovative features may not be covered. Therefore, we have set up a bonus section with a total score of 8 points for innovations such as the Tineco M12, which can function as a vacuum cleaner, and the MiBo roller-free washer or MiDu A8 all-in-one water washing machine.
