## Is There Any Hope for Survival?

"Wow, Xiaomi has launched another new product at such a low price. Is there any room left for survival in this industry?"

> As one of the struggling manufacturers, I hear about Xiaomi every day. Companies that have already entered the market through Xiaomi's ecosystem are closely watching the movements within the Xiaomi sphere. Meanwhile, those who haven't yet ventured into this space live in constant fear, worrying when and if Xiaomi will enter their domain.

While most people are familiar with Xiaomi, there is still some confusion among industry peers about what exactly constitutes a Xiaomi ecosystem company. Ordinary consumers might not fully understand it either.

"Using investment to find the best teams, leveraging Xiaomi's platform and resources to help these companies create top-notch products, and quickly positioning them in the internet market."

In simpler terms, Xiaomi invests in talented individuals from various industries, supports their product design, quality control, and sales through its own substantial size. This allows these companies to build significant momentum before launching their products, ensuring a strong debut.

Most of the products from the Xiaomi ecosystem are sold on the "Xiaomi Youpin" platform. The best among them carry the "Mi Home" brand and are also available on Xiaomi's official website.

Currently, the achievements of the Xiaomi ecosystem are impressive, with its influence sweeping through industries like a storm, leaving traditional enterprises in awe. There are already many articles online detailing this phenomenon, so we won't delve into it further here.

For those of us who are part of industries that have been invaded by the Xiaomi ecosystem, how to survive under the weight of Xiaomi's dominance is more pressing than ever. The hope of reclaiming some ground would be even better.

## Analysis of Xiaomi Ecosystem Chain's Advantages

Firstly, let's examine the image above, which highlights the advantages of the Xiaomi ecosystem chain. Compared to traditional manufacturing companies, these advantages are substantial. We will analyze each point to see if there are any opportunities.

### 1. Quality Standards

Having interacted with many friends from Xiaomi, it is clear that the quality standards set by Xiaomi ecosystem companies are extremely stringent—among the best in the industry. This strictness can increase production complexity and costs. However, maintaining a large sales volume can turn this into an advantage, as high-quality products enhance brand reputation and sales. Conversely, if sales volumes are insufficient, these stringent requirements may make the product unattractive to suppliers, leading to increased manufacturing costs and reduced supplier cooperation.

For instance, back when the Hamei phone was being developed, the constant attention to detail caused Foxconn's support to wane, ultimately delaying the product launch. Similarly, some companies in the Xiaomi ecosystem have exaggerated their stringent quality control measures during marketing campaigns, giving a false impression that perfect quality control guarantees success. However, a successful product requires more than just high-quality standards; it also needs good design, functionality, and effective marketing.

Moreover, certain quality requirements can be contradictory. Crafting a product is essentially about making choices and compromises. Xiaomi's products generally exceed market average quality levels, making it difficult for most companies to surpass them in this area. While the commitment to quality control by Xiaomi ecosystem companies is undeniable, they might sometimes fall into the trap of over-engineering their products. For example, aiming for a 99 out of 100 on quality standards can be costly and yield little additional benefit. Lowering the standard to an 85 could significantly reduce production costs without much noticeable difference in consumer perception.

### 2. Product Design

In addition to quality control, product design is another critical factor. Xiaomi ecosystem companies often place a strong emphasis on user experience and aesthetics, which can set their products apart from competitors. However, this also means that they must balance various design elements to meet market expectations while keeping costs under control.

Understanding the nuances of these factors will help other companies in the industry navigate the challenges posed by the Xiaomi ecosystem chain's advantages.

## The Design Elegance of Xiaomi's Ecosystem: A Benchmark in Minimalism

The design standards set by Xiaomi within its ecosystem are unparalleled in traditional manufacturing. The minimalist and cold modern aesthetic is a style I personally adore.

Xiaomi exerts strict control over the design of its ecosystem products, holding veto power. Without Xiaomi’s assistance, many companies might struggle to produce designs that meet current standards.

Minimalism serves as a hallmark for Xiaomi's ecosystem (similar to Muji), but it could also be seen as a limitation. Minimalist design is just one category among various styles. A fixed design philosophy can define the target consumer base and potentially limit market reach. Can a company commit fully to minimalist design as its standard and direction?

Another question arises: If three to five companies within Xiaomi's ecosystem produce similar products, all with a minimalist style, how should consumers choose? While for Xiaomi, it doesn't matter which product they buy since all are backed by investments, this decision could be crucial for the survival of individual ecosystem companies.

Traditional enterprises can introduce products with a similar minimalist design but at comparable prices. This approach would confuse consumer perceptions and challenge Xiaomi's monopoly on minimalism. Currently, Xiaomi’s design excellence far surpasses the market. However, if traditional brands match Xiaomi in design, it will significantly diminish one of Xiaomi’s key selling points.

## 3. Channels

 Xiaomi's sales channels are at the core of these businesses. The crowdfunding on Xiaomi Youpin and the official Xiaomi website allows ecosystem companies to secure significant pre-sale orders from the outset, driving substantial sales post-launch. Many ecosystem companies rely heavily on Xiaomi’s channel for their survival.

A pressing issue is that user growth in mobile internet has plateaued, entering a mature market phase. As Xiaomi continues to invest in more ecosystem companies, competition over channel resources intensifies. Failing to secure core channel assets from Xiaomi can be a matter of life and death for these companies.

Given Xiaomi's value-for-money positioning, its sales channels offer minimal profit margins—only around 15%. This means that if an enterprise tries to sell through other channels without sufficient margin to cover platform and distributor costs, it will struggle to make sales. Typically, self-branded products see retail margins of 40-50%, but with a 15% margin, there is no room for investment in product development.

Due to these low profit margins, Xiaomi's ecosystem cannot provide reasonable returns to channel partners, making their collaboration merely a one-time transaction. Essentially, it’s a business-to-business relationship where channelers and distributors do not consider brand maintenance or other issues. Xiaomi’s loss to Oppo and Vivo in the smartphone market underscores that just having a loud voice is insufficient; partners must also have a strong voice.

By providing reasonable profits to channel partners, they can help amplify the brand's reach more effectively. Additionally, offering partners adequate resources, such as a product with similar specifications but lower pricing to attract customer traffic, and then recommending higher-margin products once the customer base is established, can be beneficial.

Building deep relationships with partners to achieve mutual goals in defending against competitors.

## 4. Supply Chain

For traditional manufacturing companies, Xiaomi’s supply chain remains out of reach. Many large industry players struggle to directly collaborate due to significant size disparities—Xiaomi's sales exceed billions, whereas many leading manufacturers have annual revenues in the tens of billions.

Ecosystem companies leverage Xiaomi’s supply chain for high-quality products and competitive pricing, enabling them to create superior offerings. They also benefit from the best prices and technical support from contract manufacturers and downstream suppliers—a rare opportunity for startups.

However, how do the relationships among supply chain partners, ecosystem companies, and Xiaomi work?

Supply Chain Companies → Ecosystem Companies → Xiaomi

Xiaomi provides ID design and sales channels; supply chain companies handle product realization, engineering implementation, mass production, and after-sales service. The primary role of ecosystem companies is early-stage product definition and project management (with significant involvement from Xiaomi). Given this setup, why doesn’t Xiaomi directly collaborate with supply chain companies to reduce costs by 8-10%, pushing the value-for-money proposition even further?

Huawei’s selection process follows a similar model, directly partnering with industry leaders.

## Brand Strategy

Currently, most companies in Xiaomi's ecosystem start by selling their products under their own brand on the Mi Home platform. If a product performs well, it may be selected to carry the Mi brand, leading to sales that are ten times higher than before. The general model for these companies is to strive for the Mi brand to boost sales and increase revenue, then launch another brand for sale through other channels.

Similar to how "Mi Home" and "Roborock" brands coexist with their respective products, such as Roborock's vacuum cleaners alongside those of Dreame. However, this model has a downside: balancing product positioning and pricing between the company’s own brand and the Mi brand can be challenging. For instance, Anker's own-brand headphones sold on Xiaomi's platform are priced 30% higher than similar products under the Mi brand, leading to criticism that Anker is capitalizing on the Mi brand.

If a company's product in other channels surpasses or matches Xiaomi’s offerings, it could create new competition for Xiaomi. This might not be what Xiaomi wants. Moreover, if an ecosystem company achieves significant success with its own brand (higher margins and better performance), there is a risk of another low-margin brand emerging to compete within the same market.

For example, Roborock's first-generation "Mi Home" robot vacuum cleaner was priced at 1,699 RMB with a margin around 16%, while their standalone "Roborock" all-in-one cleaning robot sold on other platforms for 2,799 RMB and had a margin of about 45%. In such scenarios, could we see another low-margin robot vacuum cleaner emerging from the Xiaomi ecosystem?

## Market Segmentation

Xiaomi's ecosystem companies typically choose market segments with high potential but lacking dominant brands or expensive products. By leveraging similar configurations as major players while offering superior value for money, these companies can quickly capture market share.

For instance, Smartmi (Purple Rice) succeeded in the power bank market due to a fragmented and unbranded landscape. Similarly, Roborock disrupted the robot vacuum cleaner market by entering with a product priced at 1,699 RMB when competitors like iRobot and Ecovacs were charging between 3,000 to 4,000 RMB.

However, once traditional brands dominate the market without significant price premiums, it becomes challenging for Xiaomi ecosystem companies to make an impact. If their initial product strategy fails, subsequent efforts are likely to be limited. This explains why many of Xiaomi's star ecosystem companies achieved success in earlier years but have seen fewer notable achievements recently.

## Pricing Strategy

The pricing strategy adopted by Xiaomi’s ecosystem companies is crucial for market entry and success. By offering products at competitive prices while maintaining high quality, these companies can quickly gain traction. However, once the market becomes saturated with established brands, it becomes harder to break through without significant price advantages or unique selling propositions.

In summary, while the ecosystem model offers opportunities for rapid growth and brand recognition, it also presents challenges in balancing internal competition and maintaining a competitive edge in an increasingly crowded market.

## Xiaomi's Audience and Market Dynamics

Xiaomi's audience is heavily concentrated among 15-30 year-old males who are particularly focused on product performance and value for money. Any pricing that deviates from the "low" standard set by Xiaomi can lead to customer dissatisfaction. If subsequent products fail to meet these expectations, it could result in negative feedback from fans.

In a mature market, such opportunities are rare, often limited to just a few months. Treating them as the norm would unreasonably inflate consumer expectations.

Similarly, consumers who prioritize value for money lack brand loyalty. For instance, when Xiaomi's Yi Camera faced competition from 360, which offered similar products at lower prices, it had to invest in three more camera companies just to regain market share.

### 8. Marketing and Promotion

Xiaomi ecosystem companies generally allocate minimal marketing resources due to profit margin concerns, relying heavily on Xiaomi's own channels for distribution. Reduced marketing efforts can diminish brand recognition outside the Xiaomi ecosystem, thereby reducing sales.

Companies could leverage other social media platforms like Xiaohongshu (RedBook) and Douyin (TikTok) to enhance their presence and engage with a broader audience. Such promotions not only strengthen brand awareness but also boost sales for channel partners, fostering deeper collaboration.

Dyson invests heavily in marketing in China, consistently topping the market in vacuum cleaner sales. Xiaomi's value-for-money strategy has not impacted Dyson’s market position.

### 9. After-Sales Service

After-sales service is another challenge for Xiaomi ecosystem companies. Many customers report receiving cold and automated responses from customer support, which can be perceived as impersonal. The root cause may lie in the lack of sufficient resources dedicated to after-sales services.

### 10. Product Development

Most Xiaomi ecosystem enterprises rely on external manufacturers and supply chains for technological development, making it difficult to innovate ahead of the curve. Consequently, their products often focus on design (minimalist aesthetics), quality control, and higher-quality materials—essentially centered around value-for-money propositions.

Xiaomi's approach is to offer 10 yuan worth of product for the price of 20 yuan, catering to different customer segments with varying expectations. While effective, this strategy has a low barrier to entry since all core technologies come from partners, making it easy for competitors—both external and within the Xiaomi ecosystem—to replicate similar functionalities.

In conclusion, while there are notable companies like Viomi, Smartmi, and Roborock among the 100+ enterprises in the Xiaomi ecosystem, many others struggle. These companies have unique characteristics that cannot be generalized under a single label such as "Xiaomi ecosystem."

As a final note, quoting Lei Jun's famous saying: "Life is too short to worry about winning or losing; just do it."
