## The Impact of Tariff Changes on the Cleaning Appliances Industry

![trade war supply chain scenario](/images/insights/trade-war-scenario-analysis-image-01.jpg)

In my recent articles titled "Will the Trade War End with Trump's Presidency?", some readers strongly agreed with my perspective, while others found it overly optimistic. If the situation does not turn out as I imagined, how should industry players respond?

### Scenario Setup
To facilitate analysis, let us introduce two main characters: Company A and Company B, both major cleaning appliance giants in North America. They also have other competitors. We will use their different choices to simulate potential outcomes and determine who benefits or suffers.

### Year One of Trump's Presidency

#### Branch 1: Tariffs Reduced to 15% or Below
If tariffs start to decrease, it indicates a loosening of the trade war. Overseas production bases lose price advantages, so brands will tend to maintain their current status quo and not move projects. Once tariffs drop further, there might be a trend for projects to return to China. Currently, many large brands have established backup models; thus, the issue is merely where orders are placed, with minimal impact.

This scenario is most favorable for domestic leading factories, as they do not need to invest much in transferring projects. **Therefore, if Branch 1 occurs, the trade war will essentially end, and we can focus on concluding matters**. We will not consider this situation further.

#### Branch 2: Tariffs Remain at 25%
For Brands:
- Company A and B continue to transfer projects, potentially leading to a higher order volume in overseas bases compared to domestic ones.
- For contract manufacturers, most have already moved operations overseas; they may need to rent more space to accommodate increased production capacity.

E-commerce customers: These customers will also consider the tariff impact, possibly shifting orders to official websites. However, due to the relatively small number of e-commerce orders compared to those from North American large clients, there will be no significant actions in the short term.

#### Branch 3: Tariffs Increased to 60%
If tariffs are definitively increased to 60%, all orders will face transfer challenges:
1. All industries will face a surge in tariffs, leading to insufficient capacity at overseas factories and delays in delivering projects.
2. Southeast Asian regions lack the necessary engineers and project managers to coordinate these resources; domestic teams will need frequent trips.

**At this point, the R&D/project teams of major brands in China might wonder if they will still have jobs after transferring projects abroad. If morale drops, it becomes difficult to manage the team.**

### Year Two of Trump's Presidency

#### Branch 1: Tariffs Reduced Below 15%
After friendly negotiations between both governments, a larger trade cooperation is reached, further reducing tariffs below 15%. The transfer of cleaning appliance industries ends and begins to return home. (Analysis as above)

#### Branches 2/3: Tariffs Remain at 25% or Increase Above 60%
Upon receiving clear signals that tariffs will remain at 25% (or increase further), Company A and B should proceed with their plans, transferring 80-90% of production to Southeast Asia. Several suppliers also invest more in expanding overseas factory capacity to ensure order delivery.

Older models are transferred as planned, giving these companies a significant price advantage over those that did not move abroad due to tariffs. At this point, the bosses of Company A and B should be grateful for their early strategic moves, which have led to a 30% increase in performance.

Meanwhile, how to handle the large domestic R&D team becomes an issue:
- For Company A: The boss firmly believes that tariff increases (at least not decreasing) are likely. Currently, focusing on Southeast Asia is like China's entry 30 years ago; they will fully benefit from this trend. Therefore, the only task for the Chinese team is to support the transfer and train local engineers, using lower labor costs in the future to capture the market and achieve growth over the next two decades.
- For Company B: The boss is less aggressive, being a practical businessman who bases decisions on ROI. There is no difference between "Made in VN" and "Made in CN." Currently, hundreds of employees are a challenge; should they be laid off like Company A or temporarily retain them to prepare for any changes?

Since Company B is not listed, it faces no performance pressure. To manage risks, it chooses to temporarily retain the domestic team for new product development and early-stage introduction, ensuring sufficient manpower in R&D.

Due to their different handling of domestic teams, both companies' final year-end performances reflect these choices: Company B's net profit reached a record high.

### Year Three of Trump's Presidency

#### Branches 2/3: Tariffs Remain at 25% or Increase Above 60%
These branches continue the analysis from the previous year.

Due to the unchanged tariff levels and the growth in Southeast Asia's capabilities, domestic and foreign prices have basically aligned.

However, with the rise of domestic Amazon, coupled with the popularity of products such as hard floor washers, cordless steam cleaners, all-in-one docks for robot vacuums, etc., overseas markets are experiencing a shift in demand.

A Company: A company currently maintains only a small team domestically to handle daily trivial matters. All projects have been relocated to Southeast Asia for production. However, A Company lacks technical reserves for the latest market hits and is struggling with client development projects. The time cycle has extended from 8 months to one year in China to 1.5 years. They are lagging behind in new product development, failing to meet customer demands.

Local engineers have consistently failed to satisfy customer needs. After repeated project delays, A Company's boss visited the factory and questioned why they were not working overtime like XXX Company. After several outbursts, A Company realized they lacked the technical guidance for their current contract manufacturers. Much of their previously proud technology was derived from these factories. By the end of the third year, after a series of project setbacks, A Company urgently hired headhunters to recruit teams domestically. However, industry talent is uncertain about whether A Company will continue its domestic development strategy and thus are hesitant to join.

B Company: The popularity of market machines has brought new challenges. Maintaining a full team, B Company ensured the same level of efficiency in product development as before. Many of A Company's clients also approached them for new projects within a short timeframe. Motivated by bonuses, B Company's team quickly completed multiple projects.

By the third year, due to changes in external conditions, B Company's performance began to match that of A Company.

**Fourth Year After Trump's Presidency**

With the restoration of Sino-US relations to normal levels, negotiations began in the fourth year with China. Tariffs on cleaning appliances were reduced to 5-10% (even eliminated). After experiencing inefficient project development cycles in Southeast Asia, both A and B Companies prepared to transfer most of their production back to China.

A Company: Due to an unsuitable domestic team to take over, the project transfer was bumpy. The newly recruited team also faced delays due to integration issues. With poor performance, the CEO who had previously committed fully to the company was replaced by a new selection. Internally, there was confusion about the future direction.

B Company: With stable local teams and supply chains, B Company's project transfers and new mold launches were smooth. By leading in new product launches over its old competitor A Company for years, B Company became the first in North America in shipment volume for the first time.

Of course, this story is fictional. However, since the trade war began, the professional sector of cleaning appliances has differed significantly from other industries. This product specialization was primarily due to political factors rather than cost reasons, which means that once these political factors are removed, the transferred industry will face significant challenges.

**Fundamental Factors for Industry Transfer:**

1. Manufacturing Costs. Currently, manufacturing costs in Southeast Asia are still striving to match those of China. With local supply chain integration, it is possible that some large-volume products may achieve cost parity or even lower costs in the future. Even if this happens within the next few years, we will not see a significant lead for domestic production.

2. Tariff Factors. Tariffs were the fundamental factor driving industry transfer. The trade war was initiated by Trump and continued by Biden with no strong strategic intent or logical basis. Over recent years, it has failed to bring manufacturing back to America. Based on this situation, the cancellation and reduction of tariffs are likely in the future, depending on how China negotiates with Trump.

3. Technological Strength. The core reason why industry specialization cannot be sustained is that foreign brands heavily rely on domestic contract manufacturers rather than being significantly ahead of them as many imagine. Without the research and development capabilities of Chinese contract manufacturers/suppliers, it would be extremely difficult for American companies to independently complete product R&D. Once they fall behind in products, brand death will occur almost instantly.

4. External Environment. The industry changes in cleaning appliances over recent years have been numerous, making them incomprehensible even to seasoned professionals. Southeast Asian bases can only achieve simple production and cannot keep up with market trends without a parts supply chain. Retail customers are fickle; if they fail to follow new product trends, they will abandon the brand almost instantly.

Therefore, regardless of how trade wars or product transfers proceed, industry players must focus on their core tasks and maintain rapid responses to succeed in the long run.
