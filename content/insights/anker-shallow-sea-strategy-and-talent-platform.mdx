Today, I read an article on Huxiu website interviewing Anker's founder, Yang Meng. The article is 15,000 words long.
![Article image 2](/images/insights/anker-shallow-sea-strategy-and-talent-platform-image-02.jpg)
In this interview, Yang Meng systematically reviewed the history and evolution of Anker's "Shallow Sea Strategy" from previous years.

The Shallow Sea strategy was proposed by Anker's founder, Yang Meng, in 2020. He categorized consumer products into deep sea and shallow sea markets. Deep sea refers to large-scale markets like computers, cars, and CPUs with a single market size of over $10 billion USD. Shallow sea includes smaller markets such as drones and robotic vacuums, which have market sizes ranging from several billion to one billion USD.

In these environments, three types of companies emerged: Blue Whales (like Apple and Tesla), focusing on deep-sea products; Great White Sharks (such as Dyson), leading in 1-10 shallow sea categories; and Whale Clans (like Procter & Gamble, Texas Instruments, and Anta), integrating multiple shallow sea product lines for success.

Among the Fortune 500 companies, only Blue Whales and Whale Clans exist. Based on this reality, Anker aimed to become a Whale Clan in the shallow sea market.

From 2020 to 2022, Anker began fully implementing its shallow sea strategy, expanding its product lines to up to 27 categories. However, out of these 27 product lines, only seven were competitive against their rivals. The remaining 20 products lacked market competitiveness and were eventually abandoned. When Yang Meng's envisioned shallow sea strategy failed due to human factors, he experienced severe depression.

After going through a series of dark moments, Anker initiated several self-rescue measures:

Firstly, it focused on stopping the bleeding and contraction. The number of product lines was reduced from 27 to 17. All businesses not in the main trackwere eliminated, as they could not leverage Anker's existing technological capabilities.

Secondly, a rectification campaign was launched. Organizational thinking and values were unified.

First, more than half of the top-level department heads were replaced, followed by a focus on cultivating new forces at the middle layer. During this process, there was some personnel loss, but the company's overall values remained unified.

Thirdly, focusing on three major directions—energy, audio-visual, and home automation—the company deepened its technological platform at the bottom level through intensive mining and development.

During that period, Yang Meng faced his darkest moment, relying on time and meditation to overcome it. He later gained a new understanding of the shallow sea strategy: previously, too much attention was paid to logical analysis while neglecting human judgment. Now, he prioritizes assessing whether people are right and if their values align before considering business strategies.

In the future, Anker's ability to attract talent in competition with unicorns will depend on profit sharing. The company aims to allocate 70% of distributable profits to entrepreneurs and retain 30% for itself; over 800 employees earn more than one million yuan annually.

At the same time, Anker has the capability to support business strategies that may incur losses for several years, such as the robot vacuum business, which lost nearly one billion yuan from 2021 to 2025 before turning profitable in 2025.

This interview offers valuable insights into the struggles and confusions of top entrepreneurs, from confidence to falling into the abyss and then rising back to the peak. What surprised me was that the failure of the shallow sea strategy in 2022 actually led Yang Meng to suffer severe depression. The most terrifying thing for an entrepreneur is not failure but the collapse of belief. He eventually overcame it with time, and most things are not so significant; time can heal all wounds. Even great leaders go through such experiences.

As a small entrepreneur, I strongly agree with many of Yang Meng's views. No matter when or where, opportunities always exist, and whether something succeeds does not depend on the amount of funds and resources but on the core leader. Finding the right people increases the chances of success by half.

Anker's future success depends on talent attraction, which relies on a platform and profit sharing; 70% of project profits should go to entrepreneurs. With the right people and proper profit distribution, business growth naturally follows. China’s most successful company, Huawei, relied heavily on profit sharing to continuously attract talent and become the strongest tech enterprise in the country.

Values are an intangible thing that many see as a tool for brainwashing. From a management perspective, when a company grows to a certain size, physical distance makes it difficult for people to frequently communicate, leading to significant differences in understanding and judgment of simple matters. In such cases, values serve as the simplest tool to unify consensus.
