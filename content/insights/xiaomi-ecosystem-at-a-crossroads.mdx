The History of Xiaomi's Ecosystem Chain

Core Principles

- Star Enterprises: 
  - Nanenbo
  - Zimi

Currently Facing Challenges (No star enterprises have emerged in recent years)
- The era of incremental market growth has ended.
- Chinese brands are rising, making it difficult to find comparable competitors.
- The talent dividend is over.
- Xiaomi's online store benefits have come to an end.

The Paradox of Leadership (Innovator’s Dilemma)

- After becoming a leader in many categories, Xiaomi can no longer continue with its previous approach.

Changes in the External Environment

- The era of live streaming has arrived.

Xiaomi’s ecosystem chain truly competes against Costco, but in China, membership fees cannot be charged, making this path unfeasible. 
Because the Costco model is not viable, normal enterprise product models must be pursued instead.
The core strategy of "cost-effective" for Xiaomi is no longer ineffective; it just isn’t as effective as before.

- The super blue ocean market has disappeared, replaced by niche markets.
- Incremental market growth has ended, and competition is more intense than ever. Competitors’ standards have also significantly improved.

Current Competitive Landscape:

1. Foreign brands that were once the benchmark still exist.
2. Chinese manufacturers that were previously lagging are now catching up.
3. Early Xiaomi ecosystem chain companies that defected from the ecosystem.
4. Current ecosystem chain enterprises are relatively weak.
5. Cross-border e-commerce is thriving, with significant capital investment.

Keywords for Xiaomi Ecosystem Chain in Consumers’ Minds:

- Cost-effective
- Good quality
- High aesthetics

Current Solutions:

- Change the "cost-effectiveness as the only" route.
- Focus on the concept of being generous ("gan'ding").
- Increase R&D investments to make the Mi brand more technologically advanced.
- Reform internal competition to reduce internal conflicts.
- Liberate ecosystem chain enterprises.

It is evident that Xiaomi has changed. The pricing strategy for the high-temperature floor cleaner in the Mi Home store is not low-cost but:

- High configuration, no low price

In November 2021, I traveled to Tianjin on business and gave a presentation on the cleaning appliance industry to Deutsche Börse Securities. Upon arriving at my hotel, the "law of always forgetting or leaving behind my charging equipment" once again proved its validity. This time, I had forgotten both my power bank and charger. Without hesitation, I took a taxi for the first time to a nearby Mi Home store, intending to buy a 10,000mAh power bank with built-in charging cable to reduce the space in my backpack. When I arrived at the Mi Home store, I found that such products were already widely available on Taobao, yet there was none in the store.

The power banks in the Mi Home store remained as cheap as they were 5-6 years ago; a 10,000mAh unit cost only 69 yuan. So, I bought a power bank and also purchased an Apple charging cable and a charger plug. Despite Xiaomi’s consistent reputation for affordability, their products did not meet my needs. On the way back to the hotel, I reflected on the Xiaomi ecosystem chain products I had purchased over the years, realizing that during its early days, I bought many products such as air purifiers, power banks, phones, and rice cookers, essentially purchasing everything from Xiaomi. However, in recent years, I have rarely bought products from the Xiaomi ecosystem chain. Why is this?

**The reason might be that the Xiaomi ecosystem chain has become less cool recently.**

The Xiaomi ecosystem chain began in 2013 and is now approaching its tenth year. As a part of manufacturing, the Xiaomi ecosystem chain has transformed Chinese manufacturing. Before 2013, foreign brands were still seen as high-priced, while Made-in-China was synonymous with counterfeit and low-quality products. The emergence of the Xiaomi ecosystem chain changed both the Chinese manufacturing landscape and many people's lives.

Adapting to the rise of domestic brands among younger consumers today may be difficult for those who remember when imported brands were highly regarded. Back then, imported products symbolized design, quality, and functionality, while domestic ones were often seen as knock-offs and cheap alternatives. For us in the export industry, our products generally met higher quality standards and used better materials compared to those sold domestically. The reasons behind this disparity were simple: at that time, the domestic market favored cheaper goods.

The emergence of Xiaomi's ecosystem chain marked a turning point. I recall when purchasing Rongshi power banks cost around 100 yuan; Xiaomi's power banks were priced at just 49 or 69 yuan and often featured better aesthetics. The same pattern was seen with their air purifiers and other products, which followed the high-quality, affordable route. Soon after, a wave of star companies emerged from Xiaomi’s ecosystem chain—such as Zimi, Nanao, Yunmi, and HuaMi—all of which significantly impacted existing markets.

The early investment by Xiaomi's ecosystem chain focused on core talents in various fields who were given modest financial support and orders to disrupt the manufacturing sector. This was the first wave of talent dividends for Xiaomi’s ecosystem chain, with a relatively abundant supply of key personnel.

As Xiaomi’s ecosystem chain began to dominate industries, it became evident that capital plus talent could achieve such significant impacts. At this point, other capital players started competing for these core talents, making them less easily accessible.

Xiaomi's ecosystem chain had several advantages at this stage:

1. **Affordable and High-Quality Products**: Xiaomi’s strong online presence through platforms like Youpin and its own Mi.com provided free traffic to the ecosystem companies, ensuring sales once their products were listed.
2. **Strong Aesthetic Appeal**: Xiaomi popularized minimalist design as the industry standard.
3. **Talent and Traffic Dividends**: Early on, Xiaomi focused on finding strong teams in each industry, investing in them, and nurturing their growth through orders.

External advantages included:

1. **Rising Xiaomi Smartphone Sales**: The more Xiaomi phones sold, the more potential customers there were for its ecosystem companies, leading to increased product sales.
2. **Manufacturing Sector Transformation**: That period saw a transition in China’s manufacturing sector, with domestic brands challenging foreign consumer brands.
3. **Increased Consumer Income**: As people's incomes rose and basic needs were met, they began seeking better living standards, favoring higher-end products.

However, the disadvantages of Xiaomi’s ecosystem chain became equally apparent:

**Unprofitability**.

Xiaomi mandated that the gross margin for ecosystem companies be kept within 10-15%, with profits shared between the company and Xiaomi. Often heard from friends in the ecosystem was: "We don’t make money on this product, we just do it to build relationships."

If products are not profitable, why would anyone invest? For Xiaomi, the ecosystem chain served as part of its strategy forInternet of Everything (Wanwu Lianglian—Interconnected Everything), where profitability is secondary. As long as enough of Xiaomi’s interconnected devices exist, even if they lose money, the business can still be viable.

For early-stage companies in Xiaomi's ecosystem, despite low product margins, large volumes and minimal marketing and R&D costs allowed them to quickly scale sales for capital market financing and easy listings.

This appears to be a win-win situation where Xiaomi invests in the ecosystem and gains from its added value, while also implementing an IoT strategy. Early-stage ecosystem companies receive significant orders, allowing them to achieve substantial sales quickly, gaining capital recognition and potentially going public.


**Win-Win Situation.**


For a certain period, this was the case.


Just as every relationship has an end, the relationship between Xiaomi and its ecosystem companies is like a romantic relationship; it can't last forever. At some point, either they get married or break up.


Every ecosystem company wants to marry Xiaomi and become its sole partner in a particular industry. However, this is impossible. To use an inappropriate analogy, Xiaomi is like an ancient emperor with 3,000 concubines who can receive honors and live comfortably. The only issue is that they can be loyal only to the emperor and not expect his exclusive favor.


During the rapid growth of Xiaomi's smartphones, its own e-commerce channels also gained a large number of free traffic from Xiaomi phone users during this period. Entering an ecosystem company now is like adding insurance to sales. However, when Xiaomi's traffic reaches its peak, everyone will realize that the pie isn't big enough to share.


Xiaomi's dream is IoT (Internet of Things), and to achieve it, as many ecosystem companies as possible need to be accommodated to expand the number of IoT devices. Even in the same market segment, Xiaomi introduces different ecosystem companies to balance each other out. This makes the limited traffic even harder to divide.


Under these circumstances, there are natural divergences in mutual interests.


Xiaomi's orders and traffic enable rapid growth for ecosystem companies. Once they realize that they cannot be the sole partner of Xiaomi, capable ecosystem companies no longer want to merely serve as a pawn in Xiaomi's game but instead wish to firmly control their own development.


Companies like Hualai and Roborock are early examples of ecosystem companies breaking away from the ecosystem.


However, the challenges faced by Xiaomi's ecosystem go beyond internal issues. More importantly, external conditions have changed:


1. Decline of foreign brands. Initially, when we bought products from Xiaomi's ecosystem, a clear feeling was that they were exceptionally cost-effective—offering the same features as foreign brands at half the price. Recently, while Xiaomi's products are still cheaper than their counterparts, it feels like there isn't as much discounting. This is because the benchmark against which Xiaomi competes has disappeared.

2. Rise of domestic competitors. Initially, Xiaomi faced numerous counterfeit manufacturers with poor product quality and unimaginative designs. In recent years, domestic brands have achieved parity in terms of product quality and design, competing directly with Xiaomi. These new-generation domestic brands react more quickly, innovate faster, and can even be more aggressive. For ecosystem companies from the Xiaomi ecosystem, they no longer hold many advantages over these new domestic competitors.

3. Redistribution of traffic. A core logic for large orders by ecosystem companies is their low-cost, high-quality phone user traffic. Compared to platforms like Taobao and JD.com, which need to spend substantial amounts on acquiring traffic annually, ecosystem companies have a natural advantage. However, in recent years, with the widespread adoption of 5G networks and the rise of short video and live streaming channels represented by Douyin and Kuaishou, these platforms are disrupting Taobao, JD.com, and even Xiaomi's own ecosystem.

4. Private Domain Traffic. As traffic costs continue to rise, many brands have begun laying out their private domain strategies, such as WeChat personal accounts and sales by mid-sized live streamers. Unfortunately, Xiaomi ecosystem companies, due to their overly generous pricing that does not provide sufficient profit margins for private domain terminal sellers, have fallen short in this regard.

5. The Rise of Cross-border E-commerce. With Amazon expanding its presence overseas, Chinese sellers are growing internationally. Some aggressive enterprises have even achieved sales of 2 billion RMB within just two to three years in the cleaning brand market. Most Xiaomi ecosystem companies adopt a hit product strategy with limited SKUs to cater to diverse customer needs.

At the same time, Xiaomi's pricing strategy at home has become an obstacle for its overseas markets. Even Xiaomi’s minimalist design, which it prides itself on, is not the most mainstream in the international market.

6. The Vanishing Blue Ocean and Emerging Niches. Unlike a decade ago, when every untapped market was seen as a blue ocean for Xiaomi, the industry has since developed, making these large markets now few and far between. These smaller niches have lower order volumes that are not suitable for Xiaomi’s current hit product strategy.

I personally believe that Xiaomi's ecosystem companies aim to target American Costco supermarkets. Costco attracts consumers with its low-margin products and excellent service, where the products themselves do not make a profit but serve as traffic generators, while membership cards become the main source of revenue. After years of exploration, it is clear that the membership fee model does not suit Xiaomi’s ecosystem.

Since the membership fee model is unsuitable, Xiaomi's ecosystem companies should revert to a more conventional business model where products themselves generate reasonable profits. This will support corporate promotion and development.

Last year, Xiaomi launched the Mi Home Hot Water Floor Washer. Initially, there was significant speculation in the industry that Xiaomi would price it around 1500 RMB. However, the actual retail price exceeded 3499 RMB. According to supply chain data, this product has still achieved decent sales.

The launch of this floor washer marks a shift within Xiaomi's internal approach, no longer prioritizing low pricing as a key product metric.

Ten years into the development of Xiaomi’s ecosystem chain, the company finds itself at a crossroads. This juncture may guide its ecosystem companies towards their next decade.

Here are some personal observations:

1. **Diminishing Focus on Low Prices**: Xiaomi has always been perceived for its strong value proposition—wherever you choose, it's hard to go wrong. Value does not equate to low prices or lack of profit margins.

2. **Initial Advantage through Talent and Supply Chain**: In the early years, Xiaomi’s ecosystem chain leveraged talent from industries like smartphones and supply chains to disrupt traditional sectors. However, in recent years, there seems to be a reduction in technological innovation. According to rumors, Xiaomi plans to increase R&D investment.

3. **Updated Internal Competition Mechanism**: As Xiaomi's ecosystem companies continue to grow and multiply, vertical business overlaps among them are inevitable. The competition mechanism helps identify the most suitable company for product development. However, reports suggest that Xiaomi’s internal competition is more ruthless; an initial proposal by A may not necessarily result in the final implementation by A. While it is crucial for Xiaomi to select the best company, this mechanism can be detrimental from the perspective of ecosystem companies, as their ideas could be struck down by competitors. This approach may stifle innovation.

4. **Current Product Offerings**: Currently, Xiaomi sells two main types of products: 
   - The Mi brand, which is the most valued project for all ecosystem chain companies; securing a Mi project essentially means gaining a launchpad.
   - Other brands sold on Xiaomi’s platform, which operate similarly to independent sales on other platforms.

The Mi brand enjoys high sales but stringent requirements and longer product development cycles. A new tier could be introduced between the Mi brand and enterprise self-brands—products that meet Xiaomi's quality standards while allowing companies to decide on design and functionality. This would encourage innovation with consumer feedback as a key factor.

Xiaomi stands at a crossroads.
