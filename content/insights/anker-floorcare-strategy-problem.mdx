Anker Innovation has always been a leader among outbound enterprises, excelling in both sales and product capabilities across the industry. A few years ago, during the peak of the robot vacuum wave, Anker heavily invested in this sector, launching the Eufy Mach brand.

However, Anker's other cleaning appliance line, the robot vacuums, has consistently performed well, with over 1 million units sold annually. Recently, I heard from a friend that the Anker Eufy robotic vacuum business is doing quite well, so let’s take a look at how Anker’s overall cleaning appliance business operates and what future directions it might take.

Firstly, we will examine Anker's line of vacuums:

Car-Attached and Handheld Product Lines:
Currently, only one car-attached vacuum cleaner is still available on Amazon, while most handheld models have been discontinued. The company’s official website lists only robot vacuums under the cleaning category. Car-attached vacuum cleaners have been sold for about 4-5 years, suggesting that Anker may not plan to update these products soon. Given the low current sales prices and relatively low barriers to entry, it seems wise for Anker to temporarily abandon this product line in the short term. The future market for cleaning appliances is still large, but I believe Anker will only pause operations for a short period before returning when new opportunities arise.

Wet Vacuum Cleaner Product Line:
Previously, Anker heavily invested in the Eufy Mach hard floor washer series, choosing cordless steam as its breakthrough point in 2022. However, at that time, the technology was not yet mature, with poor performance and high prices for both the cordless steam functionality and user experience. I wrote an article back then explaining why I did not favor cordless steam products. But this year, Roborock and Ecovacs have started to focus on cordless steam again, showing improvements in battery life and usage effectiveness, along with decent sales figures. Sometimes, being too ahead of the market can turn one into a pioneer. Deciding how to enter the market at the dawn of technological breakthroughs is a significant test for product managers.

Currently, there are no Eufy Mach hard floor washers available in North America; they have some presence in Europe but with limited sales volumes.

Robotic Vacuum Cleaner Product Line:
Previously, Anker’s product line was more focused on cost-effectiveness and used inertial navigation solution. After the introduction of all-in-one docks, Roborock, Ecovacs, and others made significant profits. Following a change in leadership within the robot vacuum team, they have become more aggressive with their product innovations.

The current design of Anker’s robotic vacuums is quite bold, with distinct styles that set them apart from other brands. Unlike some competitors who adopt a rugged style or rounder designs like Narwal and Roborock P20, the Anker E series and S series combine both rugged and rounded aesthetics, making them easily recognizable.

From a product configuration perspective, each line is fully equipped with all features. This is an advantage of large companies; smaller companies often make choices while larger ones aim for comprehensive offerings.

The C series includes entry-level stations such as the standalone auto-empty dock C10 and L60, along with the entry-level C20. Currently, these models are priced very competitively, with single auto-empty docks starting at $249 and $299, and the all-in-one C20 starting at $449. The days of high prices for all-in-one docks seem to be over; price reductions are inevitable.

The E series also features an all-in-one dock but has made many innovative attempts. For example, the E20 integrates a vacuum cleaner into the robot vacuum host, allowing users to add a hose and brush to turn it into a standalone vacuum cleaner. This product form was first introduced by Haier for mass production.

The E28 takes this concept even further by combining a fabric cleaning machine with the station’s water tank.

These ideas are something we have also considered in our own product development, but implementing them involves significant risks due to high mold costs and uncertainty about consumer acceptance. Following industry leaders is often the safest approach.

Additionally, the S series features more daring designs, with stations that are very long and slender, resembling computer cases. Visually, they stand out from all other brands. Functionally, they offer similar capabilities as other all-in-one machines but use a roller brush for live water self-cleaning.

After reviewing Anker’s cleaning appliance product lines, here are my personal observations:

1. In the short term, Anker is unlikely to invest heavily in handheld vacuum cleaners due to intense competition and poor sales performance. With too many established brands in the market, even strong companies like Anker may struggle to find a suitable entry point.

2. For wet vacuums with cordless steam technology, I believe it will take 3-5 years before they can achieve large-scale production. Within the next 2-3 years, the European and American markets for hard floor washers are likely to see significant growth. Anker is unlikely to miss this opportunity in international markets, given Tineco’s success at home. As cleaning appliance technologies continue to converge, Anker may not abandon its hard floor washer product line. If the market in Europe and America experiences a similar boom as it did domestically several years ago, we might see a replay of the earlier hard floor washer wars. Given Anker's strength in overseas markets, they should wait for an appropriate time before re-entering the international hard floor washer market.

3. The steam products have been declining for some time, but last year, they gained popularity again due to social media influence. Whether wireless and steam technology will regain popularity depends on achieving a balance between functionality and pricing. Anker has experience in this area; whether they can continuously improve and release more useful cordless steam products is crucial.

4. For robot vacuums, the innovative E28 and E20 models from Anker have introduced several new features that few other brands dare to try simultaneously. Such innovation deserves respect. However, these integrated designs often sell poorly because combining a vacuum cleaner and fabric cleaning machine at retail prices of around $100 does not provide sufficient perceived value, leading to more customer complaints in actual use.

According to Amazon data, the E28 and E20 models have average sales performance; currently, the most popular products are still those with lower prices.

5. For the domestic market, I believe Anker is unlikely to enter due to intense competition among several top companies. The current price wars and other factors make it difficult for smaller players to profit, so there’s no immediate need to join this competitive landscape. Overseas markets remain a strong area for Anker; they can focus on growing quietly. However, major competitors like Roborock, Ecovacs, and others are increasingly expanding their overseas presence, entering various channels and even opening independent stores more aggressively than in China. This will inevitably impact Anker’s sales.

6. Early success of Anker’s robot vacuums abroad was due to factors such as brand strength, product quality, and distribution channels. Another side factor was that major brands like iRobot were priced too high, leaving room for Chinese manufacturers. After iRobot sued 13 companies, many brands stopped selling in North America. Anker and Ecovacs avoided this lawsuit, which helped them gain a competitive edge. However, with the rise of all-in-one docks abroad, domestic giants like Roborock, Ecovacs, Narwal, and Shark have become formidable competitors. Anker’s products no longer hold an advantage in this market. Continuous attempts to explore different innovative forms are preparations for finding new paths. Competing directly against these giants is a challenge Anker will face.

6. The North American market has artificial barriers due to tariffs, which led Anker to establish factories in Southeast Asia early on with their supplier, Granbo. However, changes in Trump’s tariff policies might make Southeast Asian costs comparable to China, leading to potential adjustments in supply chains. This is an unknown factor that could impact future strategies.

Anker has successfully developed some product development methods and profit-sharing mechanisms. I believe these should support Anker achieving sales of several billion dollars. If interested, you can read my article "Anker Has No Methodology" for more details. We look forward to Anker creating more outstanding products in the cleaning appliance sector.
