In northern Israel, at Kibbutz Yizre'el, Maytronics' factory is not far from farms and cowsheds.

This detail is easy to overlook. Today, Maytronics is one of the most important companies in the global pool robot market; Dolphin is a familiar name to many families and pool service providers in Europe and America. But it did not grow out of a Silicon Valley-style startup story; rather, it emerged from the debt pressures within a kibbutz.

![Article image 2](/images/insights/maytronics-robotic-pool-cleaner-reinvention-image-002.jpg)

In 1982, South African engineer Peter Rasch invented the first robotic pool cleaning device. Later, Kibbutz member Lenny Hirsch saw an advertisement for this product in South Africa and believed it might be a business opportunity. He convinced Yizre'el, who was financially struggling, to buy the prototype and rights, bringing them back to Israel for further development.

This was not an easy decision. Calcalist later interviewed early participant Jeremy Furling, who recalled that at the time, Yizre'el's debt was approximately 120-130 million new shekels, with cash flow barely covering interest payments. Roughly translated to RMB, this is about 290-310 million yuan. For an agricultural community, buying into a future of robotic pool cleaning did not seem like an investment but more of a bet.

In 1983, Maytronics was founded and the first generation Dolphin was launched.

The name Dolphin itself foreshadowed Maytronics' path. Instead of packaging the product as an underwater vacuum cleaner, it became an entry into the pool that could operate independently, providing peace of mind to users. Many years later, some customers would even give their Dolphins names. Such emotional connections are rare for a hardware company.

Early pool cleaning was still a professional business. Users relied on manual labor, pipe systems, suction and pressure cleaning equipment. What Maytronics did was transform daily maintenance from "human operation of equipment" to "robots entering the pool themselves." This change sounds logical in retrospect but required market education and reliable products in the 1980s.

For nearly two decades, Maytronics almost exclusively addressed one question: How could a robotic pool cleaner gain trust from professional channels?

In 1992, Dolphin 2001 and 3001 were launched to cover residential and commercial pools. In 1997, Dolphin Dynamic introduced dual motors and remote control capabilities. In 1999, the Dolphin 2x2 was released for large public pools. In 2002, the Dolphin Magic featured a top-access filter basket, reducing user maintenance of filters. In 2004, Maytronics went public on the Tel Aviv Stock Exchange with an initial trading valuation of approximately $26 million. In 2005, the company launched the "a Dolphin for every pool" campaign and developed the anti-entanglement swivel system.

These milestones were not like the flashy product launches in consumer electronics but more akin to a slow-moving industrial company: cleaning efficiency, navigation, filtration, brushing, ease of maintenance, after-sales service, and channel trust.

Following its public listing, Maytronics began transforming Dolphin from a single product into a global business.

In 2006, the WAVE 300 was launched, entering the heavy-duty commercial robot market. In 2007, Maytronics US established its headquarters in Atlanta, Georgia, covering the United States and Canada. In 2009, after acquiring MGI International, the company formed Maytronics France. In 2010, the M-Line was launched, simultaneously entering the Australian market. In 2012, the company supported the Spanish and Portuguese markets. In 2015, the S-Line was introduced. In 2020, the Dolphin M600 brought cloud and Wi-Fi connectivity to pool robots, and that same year, German wholesaler Bünger & Frese joined Maytronics. In 2021, the Dolphin M700 and S400 were launched. In 2022, the company acquired Australian Orimatech and purchased a 70% stake in American pool supplies sales and distribution company ECCXI. In 2023, the Dolphin LIBERTY entered the wireless pool robot phase.

Maytronics's later competitive advantage was formed through these seemingly trivial expansions. The Dolphin brand, professional pool channel, maintenance and after-sales system, long-term product reliability, and user reputationin mature European and American pool markets transformed it from merely a manufacturer into a representative company in the pool robot category.

![Article image 5](/images/insights/maytronics-robotic-pool-cleaner-reinvention-image-003.jpg)

The company's self-description has remained consistent. According to Maytronics' vision page, it aims to enable pool owners to enjoy their pools with less hassle by outsourcing the maintenance task to a suite of machines, algorithms, navigation, filtration, brushing, and service systems.

By 2019, this model was running smoothly.

That year, Maytronics reported revenues of approximately RMB 2.05 billion. In its investor materials, the company disclosed that from 2009 to 2019, its revenue CAGR was 15%, and EBITDA CAGR was 23%. The company's official website also claimed that over the past decade, it had become the market leader in the operated regions, with a global residential pool robot market share of about 48% and a public pool robot market share of approximately 44%. These figures are internal estimates but sufficiently illustrate its position: Maytronics was no longer a niche invention company but the de facto leader in the pool robot category.

The revenue structure for 2019 also highlighted its core business. Revenues from private pool robots were about RMB 1.68 billion, accounting for 82.1% of total revenues; public pool robots generated approximately RMB 170 million, or 8.4%; and safety and other businesses brought in around RMB 190 million, representing 9.6%. The growth curve of the company was always driven by family and private pools.

The real highlights came in 2021 and 2022.

The pandemic changed how European and American families invested in their gardens, pools, and outdoor living spaces. According to Maytronics' data, globally, there were approximately 28.5 million pool installations in 2021, including about 18.3 million residential underground pools, 9.4 million above-ground pools, and 800,000 commercial pools. The number of pools using robots for cleaning increased from 4.4 million in 2020 to 5.4 million in 2021, with the penetration rate rising from 16% to 19%.

Maytronics seized this cycle. In 2021, the company reported revenues of approximately RMB 3.38 billion, up by 30.9% year-over-year. By 2022, revenues further increased to about RMB 4.32 billion.

At that time, almost all variables favored Maytronics: a large installed pool basein Europe and America, increasing robot penetration rates, rising garden consumption, the stability of the Dolphin brand, and effective professional channels. On the company’s US e-commerce site, products were categorized as Best Performance, Best Seller, Best Value, Cordless, Commercial, etc., ranging from Nautilus, Explorer, M series to LIBERTY wireless series and Wave commercial series, covering different pool types, price ranges, and usage scenarios.

Optimism followed.

In its 2022 materials, Maytronics raised its revenue target for 2025 from approximately RMB 5.56 billion to about RMB 6.04 billion. By March 2023, it further increased the 2025 revenue target to approximately RMB 6.52 billion and set an operating margin goal of between 15% and 19%.

This was Maytronics' peak, also marking the beginning of a turning point.

In 2023, the industry appeared to be growing. The global pool installation base was approximately 30.65 million units, with robots entering about 7 million pools, representing a penetration rate of around 23%. From 2020 to 2023, the robot penetration rate continued to rise.

But the growth pattern had changed.

In 2023, new pool installations were approximately 575,000 units, down about 29% from 2022. Maytronics provided an income growth guidance of 13% to 20% in March 2023, but after excluding ECCXI, it was only between 0% and 6%. This means that the company's growth is increasingly dependent on acquisitions, product portfolio, and channel operations rather than natural expansion of its core robot business.

A more critical change is written into Maytronics' own materials. In FY23 investor presentation documents, the company listed three trends: Rise of Cordless, Multi Brand & Multi Channel offering, Chinese players online competition.

These three terms are hints at changes in industry rules.

Previously, Maytronics excelled in corded high-end robots, professional channels, long-term after-sales service, and brand trust. Users would listen to recommendations from pool stores, dealers, and pool service providers. Price was important but not the only decision factor.

With wireless technology, user entry barriers were lowered; online channels made price comparisons easier; and multiple brands provided more options, reducing reliance on a few high-end brands. While Dolphin's moat has not disappeared, it is being revalued.

By 2025, pressure was reflected in the financial statements.

Private pool robot revenue was approximately RMB 2.33 billion, down about 17.9% from around RMB 2.85 billion in 2024; its share of total revenue dropped from 72.4% to 68.7%. More noticeably, gross margin: private pool robot gross margin fell from 36.5% in 2024 to 23.9% in 2025.

Public pool robots were more stable. Public pool robot revenue was approximately RMB 250 million in 2025, up 1.4% year-over-year, with a gross margin of 53.8%. This business is more oriented towards professional scenarios where customers value stability, service, and long-term maintenance; price pressure is less pronounced than in the private pool market.

Revenue from safety products related to pools was approximately RMB 810 million, down 3.0% year-over-year, with a gross margin of 26.3%. The annual report noted that the decline mainly came from poor performance by US ECCXI, decreased French pool cover business, and currency impacts; the acquisition of Australian Focus partially offset this pressure.

From the company's explanation, in 2025, pressures included increased competition, a stronger Israeli shekel against the dollar, logistics and operational challenges, US tariffs, proactive price cuts to clear inventory, and impairment losses on intangible assets and goodwill.

Management changes also indicate that the company is entering a period of adjustment. According to Maytronics' official website, Oren Jacobs joined the company in 2025 as Global COO with a background focused on supply chain management, operational leadership, and strategic procurement; Amit Magen joined in July 2025 as CFO, bringing experience from listed companies, financing, mergers and acquisitions, and turnaround processes; Rafi Benami then joined in 2026 as CEO, previously serving as President and General Manager of Applied Materials Israel. The addition of a pool robot company with backgrounds in supply chain management, financial restructuring, and global technology management suggests that the issues it faces are not just product-related but also systemic operational problems.

What is most noteworthy in this annual report goes beyond just the losses.

In 2025, Maytronics' inventory decreased from approximately RMB 2.02 billion to approximately RMB 1.43 billion, a reduction of about RMB 600 million; operating cash flow increased from approximately RMB 0.31 billion to approximately RMB 0.71 billion; net debt also decreased from approximately RMB 1.84 billion to approximately RMB 1.43 billion.

This is a trade-off that comes at a cost. The reduction in inventory has improved cash flow and debt levels, but active price reductions to clear inventory have lowered gross margins. For a seasonally strong company with heavy channel stocking, any misjudgment on inventory can ultimately affect pricing, gross margin, and channel relationships.

The most striking contrast is between the goals and reality.

In 2025, Maytronics' actual revenue was approximately RMB 3.4 billion, significantly lower than its target of approximately RMB 6.52 billion set in 2023. The gap between the two figures is about RMB 3.13 billion. It did not lack growth or insight into industry trends; it overestimated post-pandemic high growth, garden consumption, and robot penetration rates, extrapolating them as sustained high growth for itself.

![Article image 6](/images/insights/maytronics-robotic-pool-cleaner-reinvention-image-004.jpg)

The four-decade saga of Maytronics is not just a tale of corporate rise and fall.

It began as an adventurous project in a debt-ridden kibbutz, evolving into the global leader in pool robots; from its first Dolphin model, it built professional channels, brand trust, and a global distribution network; from revenues of approximately 2.05 billion RMB in 2019, it surged to around 4.32 billion RMB in 2022; and then fell back to about 3.4 billion RMB by 2025.

It once defined the pool robot industry. Now, it is facing the redefinition of that very industry.

By 2026, external variables have compounded the situation. Following the conflict between the United States, Israel, and Iran, the Strait of Hormuz has become a supply chain risk center. Public data cannot yet confirm how many orders Maytronics lost due to this, nor can it prove that orders shifted definitively to Chinese companies.

It might just be an early morning call from a European or American distributor asking Chinese suppliers: Can we get the previous batch of wireless pool robots earlier? Can we add more containers for next month? If there are any stock items, could some be shipped first?

Maytronics' advantages still exist, but the pool robot industry has changed. Growth is still there, but it no longer automatically flows to the original company.
