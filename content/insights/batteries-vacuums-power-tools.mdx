Among the patents, the cyclone technology has been at the core of Dyson vacuums from the outset, while the latest V10 digital motor is currently at the heart of cordless vacuums. It is precisely because of this high-speed digital motor that the performance of cordless vacuums can approach that of conventional corded vacuums. However, many people may overlook the fact that lithium-ion batteries and battery management systems (BMS) are also core components of cordless vacuums.

Due to the significant energy consumption of high-speed digital motors, one of the main complaints about cordless vacuums has been their limited battery life. Only with the use of the latest lithium-ion batteries and BMS can higher-end models achieve up to 8 minutes of usage time, while the longest possible duration is 60 minutes. If not for the widespread adoption of lithium-ion batteries, the era of cordless vacuums would have arrived much later.

What type of battery was used on vacuums before lithium-ion batteries? Let's briefly review the types of batteries used in vacuums:

1. Nickel-cadmium (NiCd) batteries:

![Batteries, Vacuums and Power Tools image 6](/images/insights/batteries-vacuums-power-tools-image-006.jpg)

The earliest vacuum cleaners used nickel-cadmium (NiCd) batteries. NiCd batteries are characterized by their small capacity, long charging time, and short usage duration.

Early NiCd battery-powered vacuum cleaners were known for their "charge 10 hours, use 5 minutes" reputation. NiCd batteries have a memory effect; if discharged incompletely, they cannot release all of their stored energy during the next use. Additionally, due to environmental pollution concerns, NiCd batteries were banned in Europe starting January 1, 2016, from use in power tools and small household appliances.

2. Nickel-metal hydride (NiMH) batteries:

![Batteries, Vacuums and Power Tools image 7](/images/insights/batteries-vacuums-power-tools-image-007.jpg)

Next is the nickel-metal-hydride (NiMH) battery. Compared to nickel-cadmium (NiCd) batteries, NiMH batteries offer greater capacity, lower memory effect, and reduced environmental pollution. Although there is a slight increase in price, they quickly became the mainstream in the market.

3. Lithium-ion batteries

![Batteries, Vacuums and Power Tools image 8](/images/insights/batteries-vacuums-power-tools-image-008.jpg)

After the advent of lithium-ion batteries (Lithium-ion battery), they gradually replaced nickel-metal hydride batteries as the market mainstream. Compared to nickel-metal hydride batteries, lithium batteries have a higher energy density (1.5-2 times that of nickel-metal hydride batteries), no memory effect, and lower self-discharge rates. Lithium batteries primarily work by the movement of lithium ions between the positive and negative electrodes. Lithium-ion batteries use an embedded lithium compound as an electrode material. Currently, common materials used for the cathode in lithium-ion batteries include lithium cobalt oxide (LiCoO2), lithium manganese oxide (LiMn2O4), lithium nickel oxide (LiNiO2), and lithium iron phosphate (LiFePO4).


Currently, lithium batteries are generally categorized into cylindrical, rectangular, and soft-pack (polymer) lithium batteries based on their packaging.


1. Cylindrical Lithium Batteries

![Batteries, Vacuums and Power Tools image 9](/images/insights/batteries-vacuums-power-tools-image-009.jpg)

Cylindrical lithium-ion batteries were first commercialized by Sony of Japan in 1992. Due to their stable quality, relatively safe use, and high expandability, common cylindrical lithium-ion battery models currently include 14650, 17490, 18650, 21700, and 26650.

Currently, Tesla electric vehicles utilize over 7000 Panasonic 18650 batteries connected in series and parallel.

2. Square Lithium-Ion Batteries

Square lithium-ion batteries typically refer to those in aluminum or steel cases. The structure of square batteries is relatively simple and offers a higher energy density compared to cylindrical cells.

For cylindrical single cells, based on the current mainstream 18650 type (1.75AH), the energy density ratio can reach up to 215 Wh/kg.

For square single cells, assuming a capacity of 50 AH, the energy density ratio can reach up to 205 Wh/. 

The system grouping efficiency for 18650 cells is around 60%, while for square batteries it is around 70%. This results in an estimated system energy density ratio of about 129 Wh/kg for 18650 battery packs, and approximately 143 Wh/kg for square battery packs.

Additionally, due to the larger individual cell size, under equal power conditions, the consistency of square batteries is better than that of more numerous cylindrical cells.

However, due to high customizability, there are currently thousands of different models on the market, making it difficult to standardize the manufacturing process.

![Batteries, Vacuums and Power Tools image 13](/images/insights/batteries-vacuums-power-tools-image-013.jpg)

3. Soft Pack Lithium-ion Battery (Polymer Lithium-ion Battery)

Soft pack lithium-ion batteries, also known as polymer lithium-ion batteries, are liquid lithium-ion batteries encased in a polymer shell. The soft packaging material typically consists of three layers: an outer barrier layer (generally composed of BOPA or PET for external protection), a barrier layer (the middle layer aluminum foil), and an inner layer (a multi-functional high-barrier inner layer).

![Batteries, Vacuums and Power Tools image 14](/images/insights/batteries-vacuums-power-tools-image-014.jpg)

Soft-pack batteries are safer in terms of security; if any safety hazards arise, they will only swell and crack, unlike steel-cased batteries which may explode. Currently, most smartphones use soft-pack lithium-ion batteries. Additionally, these batteries have the advantages of being lightweight and having a larger capacity.

The shortcoming of soft-pack batteries is their poor consistency, higher cost, and tendency to leak.

Currently, cylindrical batteries are predominantly used in vacuum cleaners and electric tools, with 18650 lithium-ion batteries being the most common. The '18' indicates a diameter of 18mm, while the '65' denotes a length of 65mm; the trailing zero signifies that it is a cylindrical battery.

Can this explosion be avoided?

Unfortunately, it cannot be completely prevented at present. Even high-end imported cells from companies like Panasonic, LG, and Samsung do not guarantee 100% safety, let alone those using domestic lithium-ion batteries.

Previously, I heard from a friend in the electric tool industry that they had a customer in Germany who maintained an emergency response team for lithium-ion battery issues. This team is dedicated to handling various problems arising from faulty lithium-ion products. Upon receiving consumer complaints about explosions or fires involving their lithium-ion tools at home, they would immediately rush to the consumer's residence, offer compensation of hundreds of thousands of dollars, and clean up all traces left by the explosion to prevent media or social network exposure.

To mitigate such issues, major companies have developed BMS (Battery Management System). The full name for BMS is Battery Management System, which is a core technology in lithium-ion battery packs.

![Batteries, Vacuums and Power Tools image 15](/images/insights/batteries-vacuums-power-tools-image-015.jpg)

A: Why is a BMS necessary?

Q: Because the capacity, location, and working conditions of individual cells are not entirely consistent. Without a unified battery management system (BMS), it's easy for one cell to be overcharged or over-discharged, leading to failure of the entire battery pack. The purpose of monitoring and managing individual cells is what BMS is designed for.


> In electric vehicles, a BMS can achieve the following:
>> 1. Monitor the voltage, current, and other states of each cell, discharging high-voltage cells while continuing to charge low-voltage cells to maintain balance in the battery pack, slowing down overall degradation. This is the most critical function of BMS;
>> 2. Estimate the state of charge (SOC) of the entire battery pack through monitored voltage, current, and other parameters, ensuring SOC remains within a reasonable range;
>> 3. Monitor the temperature of various parts of the battery pack, coordinating with built-in thermal control systems to manage cooling for each part, maintaining temperatures within optimal working ranges.
>> 4. Detect any issues such as leakage in the battery pack; immediately alert and notify upon discovering abnormalities;
>> 5. Communicate in real-time with other vehicle systems to provide current battery status parameters;
>> 6. Record usage history data for each cell for offline analysis.

Currently, in the power tool industry, lithium-ion battery products are beginning to platformize. Taking TTI as an example, lithium-ion battery products are mainly divided into 12V and 18V platforms.

From a product perspective, the use of a common battery platform has reduced battery development costs. Additionally, a unified battery platform has significantly decreased BMS (Battery Management System) costs.

From a sales perspective, a unified battery platform allows batteries from different power tools to be interchangeable. This means that when consumers purchase new power tools, they will prioritize battery packs that can match their existing products at home. From this standpoint, larger companies are likely to become even bigger, while smaller companies will find it increasingly difficult to pose a threat to them.

Also recognizing the importance of lithium-ion batteries in the era of cordless vacuum cleaners, several major vacuum cleaner companies have started their own battery pack lines.

Recently, I communicated with some friends about the future direction of battery packs, and here are some views worth sharing:

1. Currently, lithium-ion battery packs account for a significant portion of the cost in power tools. Is there still potential for substantial price reductions? From just the cell perspective, constrained by raw materials, costs and prices are relatively transparent with limited room for reduction. The only possible area for reduction is the Battery Management System (BMS) of the battery pack. If battery packs can be standardized, BMS prices could potentially decrease by about half.

2. Currently, brushless vacuum cleaners have a high-end usage time of 10 minutes. Given the current limitations in battery energy density and technology, it will be difficult to achieve significant breakthroughs. Some factories are now testing 21700 batteries, but because these batteries are larger than 18650 cells, they would increase both the volume and weight of the battery packs, which could affect the final product's shape and size.
