## Visit to AWE in Shanghai

A few days ago, I was on a business trip to Suzhou and Nanjing. In between meetings, I made the most of my time by visiting the AWE (Appliance & Electronics World) exhibition in Shanghai to check out any new products that major manufacturers had released.

**Midea**

The wealthy Midea, a company with a market value of over 10 billion yuan, certainly didn't skimp on expenses. They took up the entire W1 hall at the exhibition. The booth fees alone must have run into several million yuan.

In terms of cleaning appliances, Midea showcased two new dehumidifiers, one robot vacuum cleaner, and a cordless vacuum cleaner.

## Kingclean

Industry leader Kingclean continues to focus on promoting its Mojie series, launching the new Mojie M12. It is clear that significant investment was made in the promotional video for the Mojie M12. However, the same old question remains: while Kingclean and Vorwerk are still insisting on upright cordless stick vacuums, most other brands across the AWE exhibition hall have already moved in other directions. This direction seems questionable. If it is indeed incorrect, then the more resources invested, the greater the potential cost.

The persistence of Kingclean in this direction raises concerns about whether it aligns with current market trends or consumer preferences. While the Mojie M12 may offer advanced features and performance, the continued focus on a specific type of upright cordless vacuum cleaner might limit its appeal to a broader audience. Other brands have already diversified their offerings, exploring different technologies such as robotic vacuums, hard floor washers, and more.

The question remains: is this direction correct for Kingclean? If it is not, the substantial investment in promoting the Mojie M12 could result in significant financial losses.

## Roborock

Roborock released a robot vacuum equipped with "eyes," claiming it can recognize various objects. During the demonstration, shoes, socks, and a power cord were placed on the floor. When I held my phone in front of the camera, the object recognized was labeled as "Shoe." According to Roborock's representatives, at present, the robot vacuum can only identify shoes, socks, and power cords.

## Tineco

The TEK brand under the Ecovacs Group has rebranded as Tineco and launched a cordless vacuum cleaner claiming to incorporate artificial intelligence.

![Cleaning Appliance Companies at AWE image 8](/images/insights/cleaning-appliance-companies-at-awe-image-008.jpg)

I checked the product, and it appears to feature a dust sensor that automatically adjusts suction power. While this isn't an entirely new function, its implementation on cordless vacuums is likely a first. Personally, I believe that dust sensors will become standard equipment in vacuum cleaners in the future.

![Cleaning Appliance Companies at AWE image 9](/images/insights/cleaning-appliance-companies-at-awe-image-009.jpg)

## Dyson

Dyson also showcased its products at the AWE exhibition. It's clear that the trend towards cordless vacuum cleaners has significantly impacted the company. Its wired models, which previously held an advantage, are no longer on display. Currently, only one cordless upright and a single robot vacuum cleaner are being exhibited.

Inquiring about prices confirmed their high-end positioning—both the cordless vacuum cleaner and the robot vacuum (originally Neato models) cost over 7,000 yuan.

Dyson likely did not anticipate that the wireless trend they initiated would first affect direct sales companies in this manner.

## Shark

Shark has also set up a highly sophisticated exhibition booth, showcasing its range of models from North America. From the perspective of product transplantation, Shark's speed is significantly faster than other foreign companies. The main issue at present might be localizing products for the Chinese market. North American vacuum cleaners tend to be overly muscular in design. If Shark were to develop a product specifically for the Chinese market, it could pose a substantial threat to numerous second- and third-tier brands operating there.

## Suxi

Suxi has seen significant growth in its non-core businesses over the years. Recent offline channel data shows that it is now among the leaders in certain segments. SEB Group has been making strong strides in Europe, capturing market share from brands like Electrolux and Philips. Similarly, Suxi has leveraged its channel advantages to gain substantial market presence domestically.

## Huawei

Huawei unveiled the Hi Link ecosystem at this exhibition. From an industrial design perspective, products within the Huawei ecosystem are nearly indistinguishable from those in Xiaomi's ecosystem. If traditional enterprises can catch up to the design advantages of Xiaomi's ecosystem, then where does Xiaomi's ecosystem hold its edge?

The question remains: if the industrial design prowess of Xiaomi's ecosystem is matched by more established players like Huawei, what unique strengths do Xiaomi's ecosystem companies possess?
