STIHL and Karcher have announced a professional battery alliance built around the ALLPRO 40V MAX platform. According to Karcher, more than 100 professional tools from the two brands will share one battery system.

STIHL is strong in chainsaws, trimmers, blowers, forestry and landscaping equipment. Karcher is strong in pressure washers, wet-and-dry vacuums, scrubber dryers and professional cleaning. They do not normally occupy the same shelf, but they serve many of the same customers: landscaping teams, facility managers, municipalities, factories, schools and commercial service providers.

For those users, continuous operation matters more than one machine's headline specification. A landscaping crew may need saws, trimmers and blowers. A facility team may need pressure washers, vacuums and floor-care machines. Separate batteries make purchasing, inventory, charging and maintenance more complicated.

![One battery connecting landscaping and cleaning](/images/blog/european-tool-brands-battery-alliances-image-002.webp)

ALLPRO addresses that operational problem by putting landscaping, forestry and professional cleaning on one platform. Existing STIHL AP batteries will work with new ALLPRO-powered Karcher machines scheduled from January 2027. Karcher also says it will continue supporting its existing Battery Power+ equipment and batteries for at least ten years.

The migration details matter. Professional users will not move a fleet simply because two connectors match. They need to know whether old batteries remain useful, when compatible tools will arrive and whether parts and service will be available.

## The battery is the entry point to the next tool

Europe already has similar multi-brand systems, including Metabo's CAS and Bosch Professional's AMPShare. Their common logic is more important than the convenience of carrying fewer batteries.

Large groups such as TTI and Stanley Black & Decker can build closed ecosystems around Milwaukee, Ryobi, Hart and DeWalt. Battery packs generate revenue, but more importantly, installed battery inventory affects the next purchase. Once a contractor or service fleet owns dozens of packs and chargers, changing platforms means replacing equipment, inventory, service routines and purchasing habits.

![Scale shapes battery-platform strategy](/images/blog/european-tool-brands-battery-alliances-image-003.webp)

Smaller or more specialized European brands face a different problem. Bosch is strong in power tools, STIHL in outdoor power equipment and Karcher in cleaning. No single brand covers every professional application. A shared platform lets several strong specialists create a larger equipment universe without each company building every category.

Battery alliances are therefore open in design but defensive in strategy. They help specialist brands compete with the breadth and lock-in of larger closed platforms.

![A battery pack becomes the gateway to repeat purchases](/images/blog/european-tool-brands-battery-alliances-image-004.webp)

ALLPRO also uses STIHL's tabless-cell technology in its Performance batteries. Karcher highlights higher power transfer, faster charging and a service life of up to 3,000 charging cycles for the Performance range. Professional buyers convert those specifications into total cost: power determines whether a tool can handle heavy work, runtime affects the number of packs a team carries, charging time affects downtime, and cycle life affects replacement cost.

The contest is simple: the battery that stays on the jobsite, in the landscaping truck or inside the cleaning fleet has a better chance of winning the next equipment order.

Battery alliances will not overturn the tool industry overnight. Product quality, distribution and service execution still matter more than compatibility alone. But they make the strategic question clearer for Chinese equipment brands that have often entered overseas markets through single-product breakthroughs: build a platform, join an alliance, or remain a lower-priced alternative?
