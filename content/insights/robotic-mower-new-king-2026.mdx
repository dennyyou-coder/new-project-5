The lawn care robot industry is entering the twilight of an old king and the dawn of a new one.

But this is not about a new king emerging in a blank market; it's about an old king defending his territory while new forces attack. In recent years, this category has been seen more as a niche segment within the Western garden tools market, with industry discussions primarily focusing on boundaryless technology, RTK (Real-Time Kinematic), visual recognition, LiDAR, and automatic mapping. However, starting this year, what is more noteworthy is no longer "whether this category can exist," but rather "who will become the new king of this industry."

This year's orders for lawn care robots have exceeded industry expectations and are even stronger than many had imagined at the beginning of the year. Now, channel partners are calling brands to find out that they have no stock. This shortage is not a problem with just one brand but a result of changes in the supply-demand relationship for boundaryless lawn care robots. In the past, the industry was still debating whether consumers would accept boundaryless lawn care robots; now, the more pressing question is: who has inventory, who has channels, and who can deliver stably.

Last year, the total production of boundaryless lawn care robots was approximately 3 million units, and this year it may reach 6 million. This growth comes not only from replacing traditional line-limited lawn care robots but also from capturing a portion of the market for riding mowers and manual grass cutters. More importantly, this replacement is still ongoing. Boundaryless lawn care robots are evolving from an "internal upgrade" within robotic lawnmowers to a reconfiguration of the entire outdoor lawn care tool market.

This is why the lawn care robot industry suddenly has capital market appeal. Some leading players in the industry have already reached sales volumes approaching 300 million and 400 million yuan, making it no longer just a niche robotics business but a potential consumer electronics category that could produce listed companies.

The robot vacuum industry once provided a clear example. In those days, Roborock was not the first company to make a robot vacuum; iRobot, Ecovacs, and many others had done so earlier. But it truly changed the industry by launching its 1499 yuan Xiaomi robot vacuum, which swept the market with LiDAR navigation, path planning, cleaning experience, and pricing that consumers could not ignore. After that machine, robotic vacuums were no longer just an expensive, unnecessary, toy-like product; they began to be considered a serious purchase for ordinary households. Roborock also quickly went public on the A-share market, with its stock value peaking at over 10 billion yuan.

The significance of this case does not lie in the fact that another "Roborock" will definitely emerge in the lawn care robot industry; rather, it illustrates what happens when a new category truly takes off: leading companies do more than just sell more products; they redefine consumer perceptions, price structures, and industry barriers. Today's lawn care robots are also waiting for such redefiners.

But before the new king is crowned, the old king still sits at the table. The core players in the lawn care robot industry have been Husqvarna and Positec. They represent the capabilities of the previous round of the industry: one as a global champion in traditional lawnmowers, the other as a benchmark for Chinese power tool brands going global.

With the true release of boundaryless lawn care robots, the basis of industry competition has changed. In the era of traditional lawnmowers, it was about mechanical structure, blade discs, motors, channels, and dealer systems; with boundaryless lawn care robots, it is about positioning, mapping, obstacle avoidance, algorithms, software iteration, supply chain management, and cost control. These capabilities are exactly what Chinese robotics companies, cleaning appliance manufacturers, and consumer hardware companies have honed over the past decade.

The opportunity for Chinese companies is not just about low prices. While lower prices certainly change the market, a deeper transformation is occurring as sensors, algorithms, lithium batteries, brushless motors, structural components, whole-machine manufacturing, app iterations, and cross-border channels are all maturing simultaneously. In the past, these capabilities were scattered across different industries; now they are converging in this new category of garden robots.

This is why Husqvarna may face a situation similar to iRobot's challenges. iRobot was not incapable of making robot vacuums; it was that as the industry transitioned from "robot inventor competition" to "consumer electronics efficiency competition," Chinese companies began to catch up and surpass them in product iterations, supply chain responsiveness, cost control, and channel efficiency. Husqvarna faces a similar issue today. While it understands lawn mowers, lawn robots are becoming increasingly unlike traditional lawnmowers and more like outdoor robots.

Therefore, determining who will succeed at this stage cannot be based solely on technical routes or a single product. What truly matters in this industry going forward is four key capabilities: whether the old kings can defend their core positions, whether new forces can turn their initial advantages into stable market shares, whether listed companies can use financial and organizational resources to continuously catch up, and whether funding-backed newcomers can survive until the industry enters its realization phase.

Lawn robots are not an industry that can be won with a single product's short-term success. It requires channels, after-sales service, local services, inventory management, R&D, and sustained price competition. Companies without real shipments will quickly be validated; those lacking financial capacity will struggle to survive until the next round.

The first category of players are the old kings, Husqvarna and Positec.

Husqvarna has long been at the forefront in boundary-free lawn robots. At its peak, Husqvarna held over half of the global market share for such machines. As late as 2025, Husqvarna's lawn robot business still generated approximately RMB 5 billion to RMB 5.5 billion in sales, accounting for about 17% of the entire group’s revenue, making it the third-largest business within the group. This indicates that Husqvarna is not a traditional company that has exited the market; it remains one of the most significant forces in the lawn robot industry.

In the face of the strong rise of Chinese new forces, Husqvarna did not choose passive defense. On one hand, Chinese brands are taking market share with lower prices, changing European consumers' and channels’ perception of the price of lawn robots. On the other hand, Husqvarna began a strong counterattack by pushing for anti-subsidy investigations in the EU. At the same time, Husqvarna is adjusting its supply chain strategy, starting to collaborate with domestic contract manufacturers. According to industry friends, Husqvarna is now collaborating with Zhongjian Gaoke.

This matter is worth pondering. It shows that Husqvarna does not fail to see the efficiency of Chinese supply chains or the pressure from cost competition. The old king's counterattack includes both policy tools and adjustments in its supply chain. Countless new kings aspire to take its place, but the old king is unwilling to exit just yet.

Positec is another kind of old king. It has always been a source of pride for Chinese power tools. While many companies were still busy with contract manufacturing, Positec chose to fully commit to its own brand and became a flagbearer for Chinese brands going global. In boundary-free lawn robots, Positec was the only Chinese brand that could directly compete head-to-head with Husqvarna.

However, as new forces have risen, Positec's business in boundary-free lawn robots has also been significantly affected. More interestingly, now almost every company working on lawn robots has former Positec employees. In a sense, Positec has become the talent cradle of the entire lawn robot industry. It may no longer be at the forefront, but it has contributed a large number of talents, experiences, and methods to this industry.

Therefore, the first category of players are not new forces but old kings. Understanding Husqvarna and Positec is key to understanding why today's competition is so intense. Because for a new king to ascend, it must not only run fast but also truly surpass the channels, brands, talents, and industrial experience left behind by the old king.

However, the existence of the old kings has not prevented new forces from accelerating their entry into the market. On the contrary, it is precisely because the system of the old kings is beginning to loosen that companies already established in the market have the opportunity to turn their early advantages into a new industry order.

The second category of players are those companies that have already taken leading positions in the market.

One of the biggest differences between lawn robots and robot vacuums is their reliance on different channels. While robot vacuums can quickly scale through online e-commerce, content marketing, live streaming, and promotions, lawn robots in Europe and America are more complex. Factors such as yard size, grass conditions, installation guidance, after-sales maintenance, local services, and dealer recommendations play a significant role. Consumers not only consider specifications but also rely on channel explanations and service capabilities when making purchases.

From this perspective, Segway Navimow, Mammotion, and Dreame / MOVA are currently leading.

What stands out about Wildland is not just its speed, but its stability. Industry insiders say that last year, Wildland reached approximately 300,000 units in shipments, and the 1 millionth unit has already rolled off the production line recently. If everything goes according to plan this year, it has a chance of shipping several hundred thousand more units. While these numbers are certainly significant, what is even more important is that Wildland did not achieve this through a sudden burst but rather by steadily advancing along its established path.

During the ceremony marking the production of their 1 millionth unit, I had an exchange with CEO Ren Guanjiao. My impression was that Wildland is not a particularly aggressive company. It does not seek to disrupt the industry with overly dramatic strategies; instead, it prefers to advance at its own pace by continuously improving products, expanding channels, optimizing supply chains, and building organizational capabilities. This style is more akin to a long-term oriented hardware company: it does not rush to tell an enormous story but rather iterates and delivers along a determined path.

Wildland is backed by Ninebot. Ninebot is quite unique; it is neither a traditional appliance company nor a typical cleaning appliance company, but rather a firm with extensive experience in overseas channels and the scaling of consumer hardware. It has ventured into categories such as e-scooters and balance scooters. For lawn care robots, which heavily rely on the European and American markets, Ninebot's channel expertise, supply chain capabilities, and overseas operational experience are crucial. Lawn care robots will inevitably face competition in terms of channels, prices, after-sales services, and market expansion in the U.S., with stable organizational capabilities and continuous delivery becoming increasingly important as time goes on.

Mammotion represents another path. Founded by former DJI product manager Wei Jidong, Mammotion chose to go all-in on lawn care robots upon identifying this opportunity last year. It is rumored that Mammotion shipped hundreds of thousands of units, even giving cars to its employees as a gesture. This detail has spread widely in the industry not because the car giveaway was particularly significant but because it signaled that the company had achieved substantial operational results in a new category rather than just telling funding stories.

Wei Jidong is a typical product-oriented founder. Industry friends highly praise his product capabilities, believing he has unique insights into product judgment. Mammotion also bears strong marks of its founder's style: strong control over product definitions, fast decision-making, and willingness to bet on key directions early in the category. This is crucial for new categories where there are no clear answers yet; many judgments must be made by the founders themselves.

What truly sets Mammotion apart is that it has demonstrated that startups can quickly scale in this category. As the lawn care robot industry enters a phase of rapid growth, speed itself becomes a barrier. However, lawn care robots are heavier than floor cleaning robots, especially in the European and American markets, where installation, maintenance, returns, local services, and dealer networks will become long-term barriers. Whether Mammotion can transition from a founder-driven high-growth company to a global service-oriented consumer robot company will determine its future.

Dreame / MOVA is the most aggressive variable at this stage. Many in the industry know that MOVA was among the early adopters of LiDAR technology in lawn care robots. At that time, a single LiDAR cost several thousand yuan and was even more expensive than many other components combined. While many companies recognized the potential benefits of LiDAR sensors, they hesitated to bear the associated costs, pricing pressures, and supply chain challenges.

Dreame and MOVA's choices reflect their consistent product strategy: elevate the experience first, then scale up to absorb costs through volume and supply chain optimization. Positive market feedback later drove them to sign significant orders with Hesai and RoboSense. This signal is crucial because it indicates that lawn robot technology is not just about cost but about finding a balance between user experience and operational efficiency.

More importantly, Dreame does not view lawn robots as a small project. Founder Yu Hao had already clearly stated in industry groups that he wants Dreame and MOVA to become leaders in the lawn robot industry. This year, the group's target is 100 billion yuan, with lawn robots being an important part of its growth strategy. For a company rapidly expanding, a new category is not just an added bonus but must contribute to incremental growth.

This explains why Dreame is so aggressive in this category. It is not simply dipping its toes in the water or following trends by adding one product line; instead, it aims to make lawn robots part of the group's growth curve. If MOVA and Dreame can achieve hundreds of thousands of units this year, they will become one of the most aggressive players in the industry.

The third category of players are listed companies with financial capabilities.

Lawn robotics is not a short-term winner-takes-all industry; once it enters the scale war, money becomes extremely important. Research and development require funds, overseas channels require funds, local after-sales systems require funds, inventory requires funds, and price wars also require funds. Listed companies have an advantage: as long as management has ideas, there are continuous capital tools for investment. Representative companies include Ecovacs, Roborock, and LD Robot.

Ecovacs' problem is not the lack of capability but strategic priorities. It entered the lawn robot market early. A few years ago, I wrote about "Suzhou's Twin Giants," which referred to Ecovacs and Positec. However, in recent years, Ecovacs has not been particularly aggressive in lawn robots. Supply chain sources say that Ecovacs produced over 100,000 units last year, with a target of around 200,000 this year. While this scale is not small, it does not stand out significantly in today's industry growth rate.

Ecovacs' real point of interest lies in whether it can mobilize resources again if it treats lawn robots as its second growth curve. Ecovacs has experienced the complete cycle from category education to price wars and channel battles for global competition with robot vacuum, and also has a foundation in robot R&D, supply chains, and overseas markets. If it merely sees lawn robots as an additional product line, it will be difficult for it to become a key variable in the industry; but if it decides to continuously invest money, iterate, and expand its channels, it remains a player that cannot be ignored.

Roborock's entry is a significant signal of the mainstreaming of the lawn robot industry. As a listed company that has already completed category redefinition in the robot vacuum industry, Roborock entering means this market is no longer just for traditional tool companies and startups but also attracting systematic investment from mature consumer robotics companies. At last year's IFA, Roborock showcased three lawn robots covering both small and large areas. Such a product lineup usually indicates that the company has a comprehensive understanding of this category. Supply chain sources also say that Roborock aims to produce over 200,000 units this year.

Roborock's advantage lies in its stability as a company. The robot vacuum business still provides stable performance, and the team is relatively stable. Additionally, with its listed company status, Roborock has sufficient resources to continuously invest in new tracks. Lawn robotics is not a short-term industry; such stable cash flow, organizational capabilities, and capital market identity will give Roborock higher tolerance for long-term competition.

But Roborock's issue is clear: it entered the market a bit late. Lawn robots are no longer a blank market; companies like Segway Navimow, Mammotion, and Dreame / MOVA have already secured certain early-mover advantages in the European market and channels. Last year, Roborock showcased three lawn robot models at IFA, indicating its determination to enter this category. But when it truly began to catch up, it faced not a group of startups still validating their products but established competitors who had already begun large-scale shipments. What Roborock needs to prove is not whether it can make a lawn robot, but whether it can leverage stable performance, mature teams, and the resources of a listed company to catch up in a market with early players, and transfer its accumulated product definition, supply chain, and channel capabilities from the robot vacuum industry to the backyard robot market.

LD Robot is another category of listed companies. It previously focused on navigation algorithms and modules for robot vacuum and went public on the Hong Kong stock exchange through related navigation business. For such companies, whole-unit business has always been attractive because they are close enough to product definition and core solutions, and familiar with cost structures and customer needs. Whole-unit business can bring in larger sales and market valuations. However, directly entering the robot vacuum field as a whole unit can easily create competitive relationships with existing clients.

Lawn robots provide LD Robot with a new entry point. It can leverage its past experience in navigation and module solutions to enter the market at lower prices. LD Robot may not become the ultimate winner in the lawn robot industry, but it could be a disruptor of price structures. In early stages of a new category, price disruptors often have a significant impact because they force all companies to re-evaluate their product plans, gross margin models, and channel strategies, thereby accelerating industry adoption.

The fourth type of player is the new forces that can still make big money.

In recent years, funding for lawn robots was very hot; as long as one was making lawn robots, it was relatively easy to raise funds. The market saw opportunities for boundaryless lawn robots replacing traditional lawnmowers and recognized the vast potential in European and American backyard scenarios. However, starting last year, this industry entered its realization phase. Investors are no longer just listening to stories but are looking at products, shipments, channels, gross margins, and organizational capabilities.

Several startups have already gone bankrupt, such as Senhe Innovation and Hesenbao and others. This indicates that the industry has moved from a "funding-friendly period" to a "results verification period." At this stage, ordinary startups will find it increasingly difficult to survive. The new forces that can remain are either those with large-scale shipments or those who can continue to secure substantial funding.

Beatbot Innovation and Aiper Intelligent are in this category. Beatbot Innovation has raised over 1 billion yuan, while Aiper Intelligent has also raised several hundred million yuan. Their original main battlefield was pool robots, but lawn robots must be a key battleground for them. The reason is simple: European and American backyards do not consist only of pools or lawns; in the future, consumers may need an integrated solution for all backyard robots.

From this perspective, Beatbot Innovation and Aiper Intelligent entering the lawn robot market is not just about crossing over but expanding from pool scenarios to the entire backyard scenario. They already have overseas market experience, high-end product experience, and certain channel foundations. If they can transfer these capabilities to the lawn robot market, there is a chance for them to transform from pool robot companies into backyard robot companies. However, the biggest problem with new forces lies here: do they have enough money, organizational capacity to sustain it, and the ability to quickly deliver products? Lawn robots are not an industry that can be concluded after one round of funding; it requires continuous investment, and the investment is heavy. The stronger their financing capability, the greater their tolerance for errors; if funding dries up, they will soon be dragged down by channels, inventory, after-sales service, and R&D.

These four categories of players constitute the main table in the current lawn care robot industry. But the real variables that could reshuffle the deck are not in Europe, but in the United States.

Looking at lawn care robots now, it is easy to consider the European market as the primary battlefield. Indeed, the European market is more mature, with consumers accepting lawn care robots earlier and a more complete dealer system. Companies like Segway Navimow, Mammotion, and Dreame / MOVA appear to have an advantage at this stage largely because they already hold positions in the European market.

However, the U.S. market may be where the next real change in industry rankings will occur. Currently, the penetration rate of robotic lawn mowers in the United States is only around 1.3%, while it is approximately 8% in Europe. The U.S. has many single-family homes with large lawns and strong maintenance needs, but robotic lawn mowers are still very early-stage products. Based on current penetration rates, the annual capacity of the U.S. market could be between 500,000 to 800,000 units.

If the penetration rate in the United States increases to 10%, the total market capacity might reach up to 3 million to 5 million units annually. This scale means that the U.S. is not just a supplement to the European market but could be the second primary battleground for global industry rankings.

This is why conclusions about the lawn care robot industry cannot be drawn too early. Leading in Europe does not mean locking down the global market. The channel structure, yard environment, user habits, and after-sales requirements in the U.S. are different from those in Europe. Whoever can solve American users' problems first may leverage a single market's growth to directly enter the top three in the industry.

For Segway Navimow, Mammotion, and Dreame / MOVA, the U.S. is a second battlefield for consolidating their leading positions; for companies like Roborock and LD Robot, which are listed companies, the U.S. may be the largest opportunity to catch up later; for new forces in the yard robot sector such as Beatbot Innovation and Aiper Intelligent, the U.S. market might even be more important than Europe because they already have channels and user bases in high-margin overseas backyard scenarios.

Therefore, the outcome of the lawn care robot industry will not be decided solely by Europe. While Europe determines who gets out first, the U.S. may determine who truly grows big.

The U.S. market will simultaneously test these four categories of players: can the old kings maintain their global status, can leading new forces complete their ascension, can listed companies later overtake others, and can financing newcomers prove they are not just concept companies?

The time to decide industry positions is likely in 2027. In 2026, brands will fiercely promote themselves in the U.S. market, laying channels, setting up examples, educating users, and validating after-sales systems. By 2027, the U.S. market will start providing clearer results: who can run their products stably, who can sell through their channels, who can handle after-sales, and whose brand can be remembered by consumers.

This is why 2026 might seem chaotic. Each company will talk about entering the U.S., lawn care robots, and their overseas layouts. What truly matters is not who announces entry into the U.S. first but who can make the U.S. market a success in 2027. The European market has already allowed the first companies to emerge, while the U.S. market may decide the final rankings.

It is too early to determine the ultimate winner. Husqvarna still retains the business scale, channel foundation, and counterattack capability of the old king, while Positec leaves behind a model for Chinese brands going global and an industry talent system; Wildland demonstrates stable large-scale production and overseas channel capabilities, while Mammotion showcases the speed and product development ability of a startup. Dreame and MOVA are using more aggressive product definitions to change the competitive landscape, while Ecovacs and Roborock have access to listed company funding and organizational resources; LD Robot may alter pricing dynamics, while Beatbot Innovation and Aiper Intelligent represent another possibility for expanding lawn robot scenarios.

But these companies ultimately face the same question: Lawn care robots are not simply a market that competes based on RTK, vision, or LiDAR technology. While technical routes are certainly important, they do not determine the outcome. The true barrier to entry in this category lies in whether products can operate stably and reliably over the long term in complex outdoor environments, whether costs can be reduced enough for a large consumer base to accept them, whether overseas channels and local services can support scaled sales, and whether organizational capabilities can handle growth from hundreds of thousands to millions of units.

The lawn care robot industry is currently waiting for a company that can truly redefine the landscape. It is no longer just about validating demand but has not yet formed its final ranking either. Over the next few years, whoever can transform the complexity of outdoor robots into products that ordinary consumers are willing to purchase, use, and recommend; who can defend their position in Europe while opening up markets in the United States; and who can survive the shakeout with adequate funding and organizational resources—will most likely emerge as the new king of the lawn care robot industry.
