Brand profile.


iRobot is a benchmark company in global consumer robotics and is called the originator of the robot vacuum by the industry. Its development history and technological innovation deeply shaped the structure of the smart-home industry. The company was founded in Delaware in 1990 by robotics experts from MIT and is now headquartered in Bedford, Massachusetts. It listed on Nasdaq in 2005. In its early stage, iRobot focused on military and special-purpose robot R&D. Its PackBot series participated in major projects such as exploration of the Great Pyramid of Giza and investigation of the Gulf of Mexico oil spill, and it also performed explosive-ordnance-disposal tasks on the battlefield in Afghanistan. More than 5,000 military robots were deployed in total.

The Roomba robot vacuum launched in 2002 marked the company's strategic transformation and its official entry into consumer robotics. It opened a new era of home cleaning robots, and cumulative global sales have exceeded 40 million units. The Braava robot mop launched in 2010 further expanded the product matrix and realized whole-home cleaning solutions combining vacuuming and mopping. The company also developed derivative products such as the Scooba floor-washing robot, forming solutions covering all floor-cleaning scenarios.

Euromonitor data shows that in 2012, iRobot's global market share exceeded 80%, making it the world's largest robot vacuum company. Afterward, as domestic Chinese robot vacuum brands gradually rose, its market share continued to decline.

In recent years, with the rise of Chinese robot vacuums, product performance and user experience have gradually surpassed iRobot's products. Under fierce competition from Chinese companies, and because of strategic mistakes, insufficient innovation, a single product line and other factors, iRobot has retreated step by step. After performance peaked at USD 1.565 billion in 2021, operations once fell into difficulty in 2022. Because it encountered antitrust review, its attempt to sell itself to Amazon failed, and annual net profit directly turned into a USD 286 million loss. Since then, performance has declined year after year, with continuous losses.

To address the difficulties, iRobot also took a series of measures. From the end of 2023 to December 28, 2024, iRobot had laid off nearly 51% of employees, reducing total headcount to 541. As of June 28, 2025, the latest headcount was 509. At the same time, to further reduce costs and improve efficiency, iRobot transferred OEM production to Shenzhen Picea. This move may save iRobot USD 80 million to USD 100 million in goods costs, and USD 20 million to USD 40 million in R&D and marketing costs.

To boost market confidence, iRobot launched eight new Roomba robot vacuums in March 2025, the largest product launch in the company's 30-year history.

But iRobot stated in its financial report that because of potential factors including, but not limited to, consumer demand, competition, macroeconomic conditions and tariff policies, there was no guarantee that the new product launches would succeed. This information also caused its share price to plunge 36% that day.

Four months later, in July, iRobot launched the Roomba Max 705 Combo robot plus AutoWash dock, the company's most advanced two-in-one cleaning system.

Latest iRobot Q2 2025 financial data shows that although revenue fell 23.3% year on year to USD 127.6 million, net loss narrowed sharply from USD 70.6 million in the same period last year to USD 22.8 million. This improvement was mainly due to gross margin rising significantly from 16.5% to 30.0%, thanks to sales of new products and a better cost structure. However, the company still faces severe liquidity challenges. Inventory at the end of the second quarter of 2025 totaled USD 88.2 million, down 13% year on year.

According to IDC data, in Q2 2025 global smart-home cleaning robot market share by shipment, iRobot, the former market leader, had fallen out of the top five and was replaced by Narwal.

Earlier Q1 2025 data showed iRobot ranked fifth with a 7.9% share, with the top four all being Chinese brands. Revenue was USD 102 million, down 32.3% year on year.

According to overseas news, in late July iRobot faced securities-fraud allegations. Investors sued on Wall Street. Levi & Korsinsky filed a class action on behalf of iRobot Corporation investors who suffered losses between January 29, 2024 and March 11, 2025 because of alleged securities fraud by iRobot. The complaint accused defendants of making false statements and/or concealing facts including that iRobot exaggerated the extent to which its restructuring plan helped the company remain stable after the Amazon acquisition was terminated, and concealed key information such as iRobot's difficulty in achieving profitability as an independent company and doubts about its ability to continue as a going concern, causing public statements to be materially false and misleading.

iRobot's share price has also fallen from a peak of USD 197 to around USD 4, with a market value of only about USD 100 million.

Against the background of pressured performance and unresolved market doubts, there are also rumors that iRobot is about to announce delisting. After delisting, it will merge with Dolphin, the robotic pool cleaner giant, and plans to build a super channel alliance covering indoor cleaning plus yard maintenance, striving to become the global No. 1. However, industry insiders are generally not optimistic about this weak-plus-weak combination and call it the twilight of former overlords.

Earlier this year, CEO Gary Cohen said that if the situation did not change, iRobot could go out of business within 12 months.

Time is tight. There is not much time left for iRobot.
