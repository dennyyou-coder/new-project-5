Uwant held a product launch yesterday, unveiling their latest vacuum cleaner — 
Uwant Martial Artist Vacuum Cleaner.
![Article image 2](/images/insights/uwant-resilience-in-china-cleaning-appliances-image-02.jpg)
The Uwant Martial Artist is a robot vacuum with a base station. This product includes several innovations:
- Self-cleaning HEPA and steel mesh filters, featuring an automatic dust scraping mechanism.
- Dust brushes equipped with green light to indicate dirt levels; the vacuum can be pushed directly into the base station after use, where the base station will suction out the debris from the vacuum, allowing for cleaning every few weeks or even months.
The most significant innovation is in the design of the dust brush. Traditional rotating rollers have been replaced by a front-to-back vibrating body, which prevents hair and other materials from getting tangled at the source.

During the launch event, Li Weijia was invited as a guest speaker. Li Weijia is a host on Hunan TV known for his obsessive cleanliness; he not only cleans every part of his home but also scrubs his shoes thoroughly upon returning home. This celebrity's image aligns well with the product being launched.

It is reported that Uwant could achieve sales of 4 billion yuan this year, a remarkable feat in an industry dominated by giants. In the cleaning appliance sector, which has been described as hellishly difficult over recent years with billions of dollars invested and many prominent companies failing, it is particularly impressive for Uwant to have survived and grown into a multi-billion-dollar company.

Here are some details about Uwant that I have gathered:
Yang Yongbo, the founder of Uwant, has a background in e-commerce marketing. Previously, he was an agent for Bissell in China, and during one period when Bislett was very popular in China, it was also managed by them.

In 2020, during the surge in cleaning appliances, Yang decided to establish their own brand. Initially, Uwant only had an English brand name of Uwant; they started using the Chinese name "Uwant" a few years later.

Their first product was a dual-cup dehumidifier.
The dehumidifier category was already a common product with retail prices typically around 199 yuan. However, Uwant launched a dual-cup dehumidifier priced at 799 yuan and sold hundreds of thousands of units in one year, making their competitors envious.

Following this, the entire industry began to adopt dual-cup dehumidifiers. From a product experience perspective, there was nothing particularly special about the dual-cup design; it did not outperform single-cup models in terms of suction performance. However, for consumers who were less knowledgeable, the dual-cup feature served as an excellent marketing point. Uwant is known to be very strong in marketing and can effectively identify industry pain points.

Next was the fabric cleaning machine.
This product line had previously only been available in North America but performed well when Uwant was selling it under Bissell. When they launched this product line themselves, it quickly became a hit in China. The entire industry followed suit, but the market for fabric cleaning machines did not take off domestically; instead, some companies began to sell them successfully overseas.

The success of these two product lines was entirely due to Uwant. Moreover, Uwant maintained relatively higher prices compared to competitors and did not engage in price wars. With the success of these products, Uwant also secured investment from Sequoia Capital.

Finally, during the mop vacuum cleaner battle, Uwant naturally did not miss out on this opportunity and launched a product with dual brushes and blade cutting technology.

At the time, the industry giants were companies like Tineco, Roborock, STONI, Haier, and Midea—powerhouses with substantial financial resources. It was extremely challenging to compete with these companies in terms of marketing promotions and product development.

For a period, I was involved in live-streaming product evaluations for cleaning machines, often interacting with these brand owners. Uwant pioneered the concept of official brand evaluation live-streams selling goods, achieving sales figures of hundreds of thousands or even millions on a single day.

Currently, Uwant's team has the strongest penetration on platforms like Xiaohongshu, Douyin, and Bilibili; it is likely that they would reach out to almost any influencer for promotion. I have an old account that received inquiries about their promotional activities.

If you look at Uwant’s product line, unlike Tineco or Roborock, which boast innovative features and significant financial backing, Uwant focuses more on minor improvements. Their products may not offer a clear competitive edge over rivals. However, they manage to sell in large volumes through their robust marketing and sales capabilities. This is why they can survive and thrive in such a fiercely competitive environment.

As the product line has grown, so too has Uwant’s R&D team, now numbering several hundred people. Some of their products are manufactured internally while others are produced by partner factories. Currently, Uwant's robot vacuum factory also provides contract manufacturing services for some international giants. I initially thought they might achieve sales of around 1 billion yuan, but it turns out that this year’s figures could reach 4 billion yuan, which is a significant surprise.

Many friends often ask me how to view the company Uwant.
After much consideration, I believe the word that best describes them is "resilient." Just like tenacious wild grass, regardless of how harsh the external environment may be, Yang and his team find ways to survive and grow.

Uwant’s efforts in both product development and marketing promotion are worth studying closely.
