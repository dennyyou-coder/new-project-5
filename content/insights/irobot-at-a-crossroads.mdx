In February 2022, iRobot released its financial report for the year 2021. For the full year of 2021, iRobot achieved revenue of $1.565 billion, an increase of 9% year-over-year. Operating net profit was $38 million (compared to $150 million in 2020), with a net margin of 2%. By region, the United States saw a 1% increase, while EMEA (Europe, Middle East, and Africa) experienced a 22% increase, and Japan had a 15% increase.

This dismal performance was reflected in iRobot's stock price, which declined from a high of $121 to $59.1 over the past year.

![Article image 1](/images/insights/irobot-at-a-crossroads-image-001.jpg)

Its stock performance has significantly lagged behind that of its peers over the past few years.

![Article image 2](/images/insights/irobot-at-a-crossroads-image-002.jpg)

Can't help but wonder what has happened to iRobot, the industry leader?

iRobot is a publicly traded company in the United States that truly brought robotic vacuum cleaners into the homes of ordinary people. It successfully went public on the Nasdaq through the sales of its products. For a long time, iRobot was synonymous with robotic vacuums.

However, as the industry developed, many companies in Shenzhen began researching and developing robotic vacuum cleaners. Companies like Ecovacs, Tineco, Aiperi, Narwal, and others entered the market for robotic vacuum cleaners.

In September 2017, iRobot sued 13 enterprises for patent infringement; detailed information can be found in past articles.

Although this lawsuit temporarily prevented Chinese companies from entering the U.S. market, the underlying reality is that iRobot has significantly fallen behind the market in product development.

iRobot's robotic vacuum cleaners have always followed a visual navigation route. Since Narwal Technology launched a laser-guided robotic vacuum cleaner priced around $1500, the industry boundary began to shift. In subsequent years, companies like Ecovacs and Xiaomi, as well as numerous robotic vacuum cleaner firms in Shenzhen and Dongguan, continuously focused on developing laser solutions, successfully balancing price and performance. This is when iRobot started facing strong competition from various opponents.

Let's take a look at iRobot's latest product line, which primarily includes robotic vacuums, mopping robots, acquired air purifiers, and the handheld wireless vacuum cleaner H1.

![Article image 3](/images/insights/irobot-at-a-crossroads-image-003.jpg)

First, let's examine the robotic vacuum cleaners. In recent years, there has been a rare innovation in iRobot's robotic vacuums with the iRobot Roomba i7 series, which comes with adust collection dock (dust collection station). This feature allows the dustbin's waste to be sucked into a paper bag within the station, enabling several weeks without having to empty the bin.

![Article image 4](/images/insights/irobot-at-a-crossroads-image-004.jpg)

![Article image 5](/images/insights/irobot-at-a-crossroads-image-005.jpg)

This product is an innovation, so Chinese companies quickly followed suit. Roborock, Xiaomi, Tineco, Narwal, Dreame, MOVA, Aiper, Beatbot, Maytronics, SharkNinja, iRobot, Dyson, Deerma, Uwant, Anker, and Husqvarna all launched similar products, rapidly eroding iRobot's advantages in the market.

iRobot'sadvantage product in the Japanese market is the Braava automatic floor cleaner. While this product does meet the needs of the Japanese market, it offers limited functionality, with only a single mopping feature. Several years ago, OEM manufacturers in Shenzhen had already introduced robots that combined both mopping and vacuuming functions, offering superior performance and better value for money.

![Article image 6](/images/insights/irobot-at-a-crossroads-image-006.jpg)

And currently in the floor cleaning robot segment, CloudWhale (Narwal) was the first to introduce a floor cleaning robot with cloth self-cleaning capabilities, elevating the floor cleaning process to a higher level. By comparison, iRobot's Braava series appears more like an expensive toy. It can be anticipated that such single-form products as the Braava will soon be supplanted by the market.

![Article image 7](/images/insights/irobot-at-a-crossroads-image-007.jpg)

In September 2021,Ecovacs launched the full-functionrobot vacuumX1, which integrates the Dust Collection Station and self-cleaning base station features, making it one of the most powerful robotic vacuums on the market. In January 2022,Roborock also released the S7 Max Ultra. Other players in the industry, including the Xiaomi ecosystem and ODM manufacturers, have been investing in this area as well. It is foreseeable that full-functionrobot vacuumwill represent high-end products in 2022. And as expected, iRobot will once again be absent from such product lines.

![Article image 8](/images/insights/irobot-at-a-crossroads-image-008.jpg)

To compensate for deficiencies in its products, iRobot spent $72 million to acquire an air purifier company at the end of 2021.

![Article image 9](/images/insights/irobot-at-a-crossroads-image-009.jpg)

iRobot claims that the acquisition is intended to complement its product line. To be honest, given iRobot's previous collaboration with Ecovacs, does this mean that iRobot is planning to release a mobile air purifier similar to Ecovacs' QingBao? If so, then Ecovacs would indeed have taken the lead once again.

![Article image 10](/images/insights/irobot-at-a-crossroads-image-010.jpg)

Additionally, iRobot released a handheld vacuum cleaner named H1 at the beginning of 2021. To be honest, if I were not told that this product was made by iRobot, I would have thought it was produced by a small factory in Suzhou.

![Article image 11](/images/insights/irobot-at-a-crossroads-image-011.jpg)

After reviewing iRobot's product line, I am genuinely concerned about the future of this industry leader. Currently, iRobot's only valuable assets are its brand and sales channels. If in 1-2 years, Chinese brands or other OEM customers bring advanced products into iRobot's stronghold, iRobot's products will be completely outmatched.

Next, let's look at iRobot's market performance (data from 2020). The United States is the main base, with sales of $740 million. EMEA sold $380 million, and Japan sold $190 million. Sales in other regions amounted to $106 million.

![Article image 12](/images/insights/irobot-at-a-crossroads-image-012.jpg)

In the Chinese market, it is no longer necessary to discuss; iRobot has basically lost its position. In the EMEA market, there is strong competition from Cecotec and a host of cross-border e-commerce players.

In the core North American market, although in 2017, iRobot temporarily pushed out many competitors, the market is not just for iRobot anymore. Companies such as Roborock, Anker, Ecovacs, Tineco, and various cross-border e-commerce players continue to erode iRobot's market share.

However, these are not iRobot's biggest threats; their strongest competitor in North America is SharkNinja, a company under JS Global.

I have previously written about the relationship between SharkNinja and JiYang; you can refer to that article for more details.

Shark is the industry leader in cleaning appliances in North America, having long been in fierce competition with Dyson and Bissell in the home vacuum cleaner market. Relying on strong innovation and marketing capabilities, Shark became the top player in North American vacuum cleaners within just a decade.

In 2017, after Shark launched its first robotic vacuum cleaner in North America, iRobot's stock price dropped significantly, reflecting the market's assessment of Shark's strength. Over the years, it is rumored that Shark's robotic vacuum cleaner shipments have reached over two million units annually.

We can take a brief look at Shark's 2020 annual report; among its cleaning appliance segment, revenues reached $1.7 billion, with total North American sales amounting to $2.2 billion (including both cleaning appliances and kitchen appliances).

![Article image 13](/images/insights/irobot-at-a-crossroads-image-013.jpg)

![Article image 14](/images/insights/irobot-at-a-crossroads-image-014.jpg)

IRobot was not content to let Shark easily enter its core market.

In 2019, IRobot resorted to an old tactic and filed a complaint with the International Trade Commission (ITC) to ban the import and sale of Shark's products. But this Shark was no longer the weakling it once was; it did not get intimidated by IRobot and instead directly faced off against IRobot in the ITC, as a philosopher once said, "Shark is no pushover."

After over two years of legal battles, JS Global Life announced on October 2021:

>The U.S. Patent Trial and Appeal Board (PTAB) has again ruled that one of iRobot's six patent infringement claims against SharkNinja for its robotic vacuum cleaner is invalid. This follows earlier rulings by the PTAB declaring three other patents invalid in this case, as well as IRobot's voluntary withdrawal of two other patent claims it had filed. The latest ruling means that SharkNinja has won a significant victory in this over-two-year-long dispute over robotic vacuum cleaner patents, with all of iRobot's litigation requests being rejected.
>
> In this case, the core infringement allegations for all six patents have been declared invalid or dismissed by the PTAB, leaving only a weak claim of false advertising. SharkNinja is confident it will win on this remaining charge because IRobot has failed to secure any victories in its patent cases over the past two years, with all of its litigation requests being rejected.

If IRobot can no longer rely on patents to block competitors from entering the market, where might the once-leading company go?

IRobot stands at a crossroads.
