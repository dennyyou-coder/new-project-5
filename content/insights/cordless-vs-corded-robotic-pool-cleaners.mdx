**Neither cordless nor corded robotic pool cleaners are better for every pool. Cordless models remove the floating operating cable, but they add a sealed battery, charging and retrieval workflow. Corded models use continuous external low-voltage power, but they add a cable and poolside power system. The correct choice depends on the exact pool, cleaning cycle, operating labor, parts support, warranty and intended use.**

For a homeowner, the visible difference is cable versus battery. For a distributor, importer, pool professional or facility operator, the decision is a complete operating architecture. It affects how the robot is deployed, how often it can run, which components can fail, what must be stocked and who pays when the product cannot complete the job.

This comparison does not declare one universal winner. It explains what each architecture changes—and what buyers should prove before accepting either system.

## Cordless vs Corded Robotic Pool Cleaners at a Glance

| Decision area | Corded robotic pool cleaner | Cordless robotic pool cleaner |
|---|---|---|
| Energy | Poolside power supply sends low-voltage power through a floating cable | Sealed onboard battery powers the robot; charger or dock replenishes it outside the cleaning cycle |
| Runtime | No onboard battery-capacity limit, but cycle length, duty and cleaning pauses remain model-specific | Each cycle depends on battery capacity, mode, temperature, load and battery condition |
| Daily handling | Deploy, route and store the cable; protect and position the power supply | Retrieve, drain, dry as instructed, charge and redeploy the robot |
| Automation | Selected models can run scheduled repeat cycles while connected to external power | Scheduling depends on remaining energy and whether the robot can recharge without manual retrieval |
| Main service path | Cable, swivel, connector, power supply, pump, drive and wear parts | Battery, BMS, seals, charger or dock, contacts, pump, drive and wear parts |
| Logistics | No onboard lithium-battery transport chain in the typical corded architecture | Lithium-battery testing, documentation, packaging and carrier rules become part of the supply chain |
| Best fit | Projects that value repeated cycles and can manage a safe cable and power layout | Projects that value cable-free deployment and can support charging and retrieval labor |

The table is an architecture guide, not a product verdict. Current premium cordless products can clean floors, walls and waterlines and run for several hours. Current corded products range from simple floor cleaners to connected residential and heavy-duty commercial systems. The exact SKU matters more than the label.

For the pumps, brushes, internal filters and navigation behind both systems, see [How Do Robotic Pool Cleaners Work?](https://worldcleanbiz.com/blog/how-robotic-pool-cleaners-work). This article focuses on the next decision: which energy and operating system fits the job.

## What Does Corded or Cordless Actually Mean?

A corded robotic pool cleaner normally connects to a poolside power supply through a floating low-voltage cable. The robot still carries its own drive, pump, brushes, filter and control system. The cable supplies energy; it does not turn the robot into a suction-side cleaner that depends on the pool’s circulation pump.

A cordless robotic pool cleaner carries a rechargeable battery inside the wet product. Its charger, charging cable or dock remains outside the cleaning mission. Some cordless products must be removed after every cycle. Others offer multi-cycle modes, waterline parking, retrieval features or more automated charging arrangements.

Maytronics’ current [category explanation](https://www.maytronics.com/en-ca/what-is-a-robotic-pool-cleaner.html) makes this distinction directly: corded products use a floating low-voltage cable and power supply, while cordless products use a rechargeable lithium-ion battery. The same manufacturer currently sells both architectures. That is useful evidence that supply architecture and brand identity are separate questions.

## Do Corded Pool Robots Clean Better Than Cordless Models?

Not as a category rule.

Cleaning performance comes from the complete machine and the pool it is asked to clean: pump flow, brush contact, filtration, traction, navigation, cycle design, debris load, surface, geometry and maintenance condition. Energy architecture influences the design constraints, but it does not settle the result by itself.

For example, Maytronics currently lists its corded [Dolphin Explorer E50](https://maytronics.com/en-us/store/residential-pools/best-performance-cleaners/dolphin-explorer-e50/99996281-XP.html) for floor, wall and waterline coverage with several cycle options and a weekly timer. The same company lists its cordless [Dolphin LIBERTY 600](https://www.maytronics.com/en-us/residential-pools/best-performance-cleaners/dolphin-liberty-600_99998600-US.html) for floor, wall and waterline coverage, a default three-hour cycle and up to 4.5 hours of operation.

Those are manufacturer specifications for two named U.S. products, not a controlled test. They prove that both architectures can support high-coverage feature sets. They do not prove that every corded or cordless model performs the same way.

Professional comparisons should use a representative pool and debris mix. Test:

- the floor, walls, waterline, slopes, transitions and declared special zones;
- leaves, sand, fine particles and the debris that actually enters the site;
- a clean filter and a filter approaching the agreed service point;
- the normal mode and any high-power or extended mode included in the purchase; and
- one complete mission, including retrieval or return, not one successful climb.

Published flow rates, motor counts, microns and maximum runtime should remain separate fields. Without the same test method and conditions, they cannot be converted into a reliable cross-brand score.

## Runtime Is Not the Same as Operating Capacity

Runtime is one cycle-level input. Operating capacity is how much verified work the system can complete over a day or week with the available labor, charging, cleaning and maintenance process.

A corded robot does not have an onboard battery limit, but it still follows programmed cycles. Filters fill, cables need inspection and arrangement, robots require removal or service, and residential products may not be approved for high-duty use. “External power” should not be translated into “continuous operation without limits.”

Selected corded Dolphin models illustrate the scheduling opportunity. Maytronics’ current [weekly-timer guidance](https://www.maytronics.com/global/support/faq/?question=how-do-i-set-the-weekly-timer-for-my-dolphin) says supported products can run programmed cycles during the week while the power supply remains on. The guidance also tells the owner to verify that the exact model supports the feature.

A cordless robot has a finite energy budget for each charge. The published maximum may belong to one mode rather than full floor-wall-waterline cleaning. Temperature, pump load, navigation, battery age and debris conditions can change the usable margin. A 180-minute specification does not prove that a robot will finish a particular pool in 180 minutes.

Cordless capacity should therefore be calculated as a workflow:

> **Completed missions per day**
> = available charged units
> × missions each unit can finish
> − retrieval, drainage, charging, filter cleaning and exception time

For a single residential pool, that workflow may be simple. For a service contractor moving between sites or a facility running several cycles, the battery, charger, spare-unit and labor plan becomes part of capacity.

## Which Architecture Is More Convenient?

Convenience depends on which task the operator wants to remove.

Cordless removes the cable from the water. That can reduce cable routing, tangling concerns and visual obstruction during the cycle. It can also make it easier to move one unit between suitable pools. The operator still has to manage the wet product: lift or retrieve it, let water drain, clean the filter, follow the charging instructions and return it to service.

Corded removes the charging cycle from the machine. A selected scheduled model can be ready for repeat operation while connected. The operator still has to position the power supply, deploy only the needed cable, inspect the cord and connector, remove the robot when required and store the system correctly.

The labor comparison should be measured, not described with adjectives. During a site trial, record:

- minutes to deploy the complete system;
- interventions during the cleaning cycle;
- minutes to retrieve and drain the robot;
- cable arrangement or charging work;
- filter cleaning and consumable work; and
- time until the system is ready for its next required mission.

A cable-free product can require more post-cycle handling. A corded product can require more site preparation. Either can be the lower-labor option for the right operating pattern.

## Cable Problems and Battery Problems Are Different Service Systems

Both architectures use pumps, drive components, brushes, tracks or wheels, filters, seals and electronics. Their energy paths add different service items.

### Corded service path

Buyers should identify the floating cable, swivel where used, connectors, power supply and destination plug or protective-device instructions. Ask whether the cable, swivel and power supply are separate service parts; how connector sealing is restored; and which repairs require an authorized center.

Current Maytronics listings show model-specific replacement power supplies and compatibility records. That is a useful service pattern: the word “power supply” is not enough. The exact part number and compatible robot list determine whether inventory will solve a field failure.

### Cordless service path

Buyers should identify the battery pack, battery-management system, wet-side enclosure and seals, charger or dock, charging contacts or inductive interface and required post-repair waterproofing test. A statement that the product contains a lithium-ion battery does not explain whether the pack can be replaced locally or whether the entire robot must travel to a service center.

Warranty language can expose this difference. Beatbot’s current [warranty policy](https://beatbot.com/pages/warranty-policy) places battery, PCB, motor and sensors within named main-body coverage for listed products, provides separate treatment for consumables and describes shipping to Beatbot or a designated service center. Polaris’ current [limited warranty](https://www.fluidrausa.com/-/media/zodiac/global/downloads/corporate/h0355004.pdf?rev=-1) separately lists robotic cleaners, battery-powered robotic cleaners, commercial robotic cleaners and wear items.

Neither document supplies a universal warranty for the category. They show why buyers need the exact seller, region, product, use and component terms before calculating lifecycle cost.

The longer maintenance question is covered in [How Long Do Robotic Pool Cleaners Last?](https://worldcleanbiz.com/blog/how-long-do-robotic-pool-cleaners-last). For this comparison, the critical question is whether the selected architecture has a practical repair route in the destination market.

## Cordless Pool Robots Add a Lithium-Battery Logistics Chain

A cordless pool robot is also equipment containing a lithium battery. Importers and distributors need the battery identity, watt-hour rating, applicable test summary, packaging configuration and transport route before shipment.

The current [UNECE Manual of Tests and Criteria files](https://unece.org/transport/dangerous-goods/rev8-files) include the lithium-battery tests in subsection 38.3. The [IATA battery guidance](https://www.iata.org/contentassets/05e6d8742b0047259bf3a700bc9d42b9/lithium-battery-guidance-document.pdf) explains that air-transport treatment depends on factors including battery type, watt-hours, whether batteries are contained in equipment or packed with it, packaging and operator variations.

A UN 38.3 test summary is important, but it is not a global product-approval certificate. It does not replace destination-market electrical, radio, chemical, environmental, labeling or product-safety work, and it does not guarantee that every carrier will accept every shipment route.

Battery control also continues after sale. The U.S. Consumer Product Safety Commission’s April 2026 [WYBOTICS recall](https://www.cpsc.gov/Recalls/2026/Wybotics-Recalls-Robotic-Pool-Vacuums-Due-to-Burn-and-Fire-Hazards) covered specified Osprey 700 Max and S1 model and serial ranges because their lithium-ion batteries could overheat. The notice named Wybotics Co. Ltd. as manufacturer and Wybotics Inc. as U.S. importer.

That recall is not evidence that every WYBOT product—or cordless pool robots generally—is unsafe. It demonstrates why a buyer needs model and serial traceability, the correct manufacturer and importer identity, a stop-use channel and a funded remedy process.

## Is Corded or Cordless Better for Commercial Pools?

The word “commercial” cannot be assigned by power architecture.

Maytronics currently markets a dedicated [WAVE commercial range](https://www.maytronics.com/global/robots-commercial-pools.html) for facilities including hotels, resorts, fitness centers, water parks and large pools. That product line exists because commercial pool size, duty, support and operating requirements differ from ordinary residential use.

At the same time, a feature-rich cordless residential robot is not automatically authorized for hotel, school, gym, public-pool or rental operation. Beatbot’s current standard warranty excludes commercial, industrial, rental and other non-household use unless expressly authorized in writing.

A professional buyer should request:

- written intended-use authorization for the exact SKU;
- daily and weekly duty assumptions;
- commercial warranty and exclusions;
- local service capacity and repair turnaround;
- spare robot, battery, charger, power supply and wear-part policy;
- software, account and connectivity support; and
- responsibility for downtime, freight and safety action.

Corded may be a practical starting point for repeated externally powered cycles. Cordless may be useful where removing the operating cable solves an important handling problem. Only a commercial product and service agreement can turn either starting point into a supportable system.

## How Should Buyers Compare Total Cost?

Purchase price misses the operating system around the robot.

> **Installed lifecycle cost**
> = robot + power supply / charger / dock + accessories
> + operator labor per cycle + energy + consumables
> + cable / battery / seal / drive / pump service
> + spare inventory + freight + warranty handling
> + downtime + replacement or disposal

The formula should use local evidence. Labor rates, electricity, taxes, battery shipping, service-center distance and pool conditions vary too widely for one universal price conclusion.

Ask the supplier to price the expected service case, not only the first carton. For corded products, include power supply and cable replacement. For cordless products, include battery and charger policy, waterproof repair capability and regulated transport. For both, include filters, brushes, tracks, seals, pumps, labor and the cost of a machine that is unavailable during the swimming season.

## A B2B Procurement and Acceptance Checklist

![Robotic pool cleaner procurement workflow covering site, operations, lifecycle, responsibility and acceptance](/images/blog/pool-robot-procurement-acceptance-matrix.webp)

### Product identity

- Record the complete robot, power supply or charger, battery, dock, App and accessory SKUs.
- Confirm the destination-market version, plug, radio configuration and language set.
- Identify the brand owner, legal manufacturer, factory, importer, contract seller, service provider, warranty provider and recall contact separately.

### Site and mission

- Document pool length, area, depth, shape, surface, slope, drains, steps, platforms and waterline.
- Define the debris mix, required zones, cycle frequency and acceptable manual intervention.
- Test a complete representative mission, including retrieval or return and readiness for the next cycle.

### Architecture-specific evidence

- For corded products, verify cable length and routing, power-supply placement, connector and cord inspection, destination protective-device instructions and service parts.
- For cordless products, verify mode-specific runtime, charge time, retrieval, drainage and charging steps, battery service, sealing controls and transport documentation.
- Do not accept a maximum figure without its mode, conditions and product version.

### Lifecycle and commercial use

- Obtain part numbers, prices, lead times and repair procedures for the battery or power supply, cable, seals, pump, drive, brushes, tracks and filters.
- Confirm whether a wet-side repair requires a leak or waterproofing test and who can perform it.
- Obtain written commercial-use approval, warranty, service level and spare-unit plan where the product will not be used as an ordinary household appliance.

## FAQ

### Are cordless robotic pool cleaners better than corded models?

Not universally. Cordless removes the operating cable but adds a battery, charging and retrieval system. Corded removes the onboard battery limit but adds a floating cable and poolside power system. Compare the exact pool, mission, labor and service route.

### Are corded pool robots more powerful?

Do not assume so from the cable alone. External power changes the energy constraint, but cleaning depends on the pump, brushes, filtration, traction, navigation, mode and pool conditions. Compare products under the same test.

### How long do cordless pool robots run?

It varies by product and mode. A published maximum may apply only to floor cleaning, skimming or an economy mode. Verify the default full-pool cycle, charge time, battery condition and real site.

### Can a corded pool robot run every day?

Selected products support weekly schedules, but the exact manual defines the cycle, pool-storage, filter-cleaning and inspection requirements. Residential products should not be treated as unlimited-duty commercial equipment.

### Do cordless pool robots need to be removed after every cycle?

Many require retrieval for cleaning and charging. Some support multi-cycle modes, parking, recall or more automated charging arrangements. Confirm the exact workflow rather than assuming all cordless models operate the same way.

### Which system is cheaper to maintain?

There is no category-wide answer. Corded systems can need cables, swivels and power supplies; cordless systems can need batteries, chargers, docks and sealing work. Parts availability, local repair, freight and downtime determine the result.

### Are cordless robotic pool cleaners suitable for commercial pools?

Only when the exact product and contract authorize that use. Do not extend a residential specification or warranty to a hotel, school, gym, public pool, service business or rental fleet without written confirmation.

### What documents should an importer request for a cordless pool robot?

Request the complete battery identity and watt-hours, applicable UN 38.3 test summary, transport and packaging information, and the destination-market product documentation. Have the logistics provider confirm the exact shipping route and carrier rules.

## Final Takeaway

The most visible difference is the cable. The more important difference is the operating system around it.

A cordless robot exchanges the floating cable for a battery, charger and retrieval cycle. A corded robot exchanges battery limits for an external power supply and cable-management system. Both can deliver capable pool cleaning; both can fail when the site, labor, parts or intended use are mismatched.

Choose the architecture only after the exact product completes the real mission and the supplier proves how it will be powered, handled, repaired, transported, warranted and supported.
