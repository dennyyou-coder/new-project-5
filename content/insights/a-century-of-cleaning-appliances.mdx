In 1860, Daniel Hess from Iowa, USA, invented a manual vacuum cleaner.

![Original image 1](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-001.jpg)

1883, German brothers Carl and Adolf Vorwerk founded the company Vorwerk (Vorwerk). The company has since grown rapidly in the vacuum cleaner industry by virtue of its superior quality and unique direct sales model.

![Original image 2](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-002.jpg)

In 1883, Melville Reuben Bissell and Ann Bissell formally established the Bissell Company to sell carpet sweepers.

![Original image 3](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-003.jpg)

![Original image 4](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-004.jpg)

In 1884, Robert Weintraud founded Robert Weintraud GmbH & Co KG, and formed the brand Rowenta (known in Chinese as "Rowenta") from the letters of his name. Now, Rowenta is one of the most important brands under the SEB Group of France.

![Original image 5](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-005.jpg)

In 1889, Melville Bissell, aged 49, died of pneumonia, leaving behind five children and the company. His wife, Anna Bissell, took over from her husband and successfully transformed Bissell from a small company into an industry giant.

![Original image 6](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-006.jpg)

In 1899, the German company Miele & Cie was founded and released its first vacuum cleaner in 1927. This company, known as Meinō in Chinese, later became synonymous with high-end vacuum cleaners.

![Original image 7](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-007.jpg)

In 1905, Philip Geier founded P.A. Geier Company, which was later renamed Royal Appliance Manufacturing in 1953. The most famous brand under the Royal company was Dirt Devil. The company was later acquired by TTI.

![Original image 8](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-008.jpg)

1906, Peder Andersen Fisker and Hans Marius Nielsen founded Nilfisk A/S in Denmark. The name is derived from the founders' names: Nil (from Nielesen) and Fisker's fisk. In continental Europe, Nilfisk was the first company to sell vacuum cleaners.

![Original image 9](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-009.jpg)

In 1906, James Kirby founded the Kirby Company, which has been in operation for over a century. Currently, Kirby's vacuum cleaner products are sold through direct sales.

![Original image 10](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-010.jpg)

In 1908, Henry Hoover purchased the patent from Spangler and founded the Hoover Company, releasing the Model O vacuum cleaner. Currently, the Hoover brand remains an important presence in the North American market.

![Original image 11](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-011.jpg)

In 1910, Nilfisk produced the first vacuum cleaner in continental Europe, C1.

![Original image 12](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-012.jpg)

In 1919, Electrolux began producing vacuum cleaners, then known as Lux AB. Through numerous acquisitions, Electrolux became the leading vacuum cleaner brand in the European market and went on to become a multinational household appliance giant through a series of mergers and acquisitions.

![Original image 13](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-013.jpg)

In 1953, the British company ShopVac was founded, which would later become animportant playerin the industry of bucket-style dry and hard floor washers.

![Original image 14](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-014.jpg)

1954, engineers Shen Yanmin and others from the privately-owned Shanghai Yongtaihe Electric Factory developed 200W and 400W upright vacuum cleaners by referring to foreign prototypes. In 1955, they further developed a 200W DC-powered vacuum cleaner, primarily for use on international trains traveling between China and the Soviet Union, with a total production of 372 vacuum cleaners.

1956, Bissell introduced the manual carpet shampooer, which resulted in a fivefold increase in Bissell's sales.

![Original image 15](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-015.jpg)

In 1958, Nilfisk began producing vacuum cleaners with dust bags G70.

![Original image 16](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-016.jpg)

In 1966, the Dahua Hardware Factory was renamed Shanghai Electrical Three Plant and began producing vacuum cleaners under the "Joyful Brand," with an workforce of 115 people. In 1968, 2,627 vacuum cleaners were manufactured.

![Original image 17](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-017.jpg)

In 1967, Robert Bosch GmbH (Robert Bosch) and Siemens AG (Siemens) jointly established BSH Home Appliances (BSH Home Appliances). Leveraging its own strength, BSH became the largest home appliance company in Europe. In 2014, Robert Bosch acquired a 50% stake in Siemens, making BSH a wholly-owned subsidiary.

![Original image 18](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-018.jpg)

In 1977, Alan Brazier founded the VAX company in the UK, and at one point, VAX was the most popular vacuum cleaner brand in Britain.

![Original image 19](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-019.jpg)

In 1979, Black & Decker officially released the Dustbuster wireless handheld vacuum cleaner, which is believed to be the world's first wireless vacuum. It sold one million units that year. The Dustbuster wireless vacuum was developed by Black & Decker for NASA’s Apollo moon landing mission, intended for use by astronauts in space.

![Original image 20](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-020.jpg)

Between 1979 and 1984, Dyson experimented with 5,127 prototypes over a span of four years and finally invented the first bagless vacuum cleaner. Dyson brought his new invention to then-famous vacuum manufacturers such as Black & Decker, Electrolux, and Hoover. At that time, all of these companies' products were dustbag models, with dustbags being an important source of their profits. Therefore, they would have no interest in distributing a product that could disrupt their business model.

![Original image 21](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-021.jpg)

In 1981 at a trade show in Lisbon, Numatic showcased their latest canister vacuum. Their engineers, bored during the demonstration, drew smiles on the machines as a lighthearted touch. Little did they know that these smiling Henry vacuums would become incredibly popular, marking the birth of a memorable design.

![Original image 22](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-022.jpg)

In 1985, Japanese company Apex contacted James Dyson and improved the first-generation machine DC02 on a new one. This pink machine sold for as much as $2000 but remained popular and won various design awards.

![Original image 23](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-023.jpg)

In 1985, Hitachi demonstrated a sample of an indoor cleaning robot, HCR-00, at the Domotech exhibition. This machine featured a gyroscope and ultrasonic radar for positioning.

![Original image 24](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-024.jpg)

In 1985, Techtronic Industries (TTI), invested with two million US dollars, was formally established. When naming the company, Cheng Zhiping drew inspiration from his former employer's name, ITT, and chose TTI as the name. Horst Pudwill was responsible for sales, while Cheng Zhiping managed the factory.

![Original image 25](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-025.jpg)

In 1986, Suzhou Vacuum Cleaner Factory produced the first generation of Chinese household vacuum cleaners, specifically the upright Spring Flower brand model. The Spring Flower vacuum cleaner, like the Great Wall electric fan, Snow Sea refrigerator, and Peacock television, became popular throughout China, with Suzhou people proudly referring to them as the "Four Great Dan."

![Original image 26](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-026.jpg)

In 1987, Ni Zuguang (who later became the boss of Leck Electrical) was transferred from military service and joined Suzhou Vacuum Cleaner Factory at the end of the year. He served as a motor engineer, deputy plant manager of the Motor Plant, and vice-plant manager of the main factory.

![Original image 27](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-027.jpg)

On April 12, 1988, Chunhua Vacuum Cleaner Factory was established. It comprised seven sub-factories for motor manufacturing, injection molding, stamping processing, packaging materials, accessory assembly, and final product assembly. In that year, Chunhua vacuum cleaners produced reached 899,500 units. Chunhua Electrical Appliances is regarded as thetraining groundof the vacuum cleaner industry, with many prominent figures in the vacuum cleaner sector having direct ties to it.

![Original image 28](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-028.jpg)

In 1988, Ryobi from Japan came to Chinese factories in search of contract manufacturers for its power tool business. After negotiations, Ryobi invested 20% and began placing orders with Tristar Industrial Co., Ltd. Through its collaboration with Ryobi, TTI's quality and technology significantly improved, and brands such as Bissell and Royal also became customers of TTI.

![Original image 29](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-029.jpg)

In 1988, the French small appliance giant SEB Group acquired the German Rowenta brand. In recent years, Rowenta-branded vacuum cleaners have been making inroads in Europe, securing a significant share of the market.

![Original image 30](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-030.jpg)

In 1999, recognizing the limitations of its contract manufacturing business, Pudwill prepared for a transition by acquiring the British vacuum cleaner brand Vax from VAX International Ltd. for approximately $700,000.

![Original image 31](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-031.jpg)

In 1990, Ecovacs founder Qian Dongqi began to engage with the vacuum cleaner industry. Since then, he officially entered into contract manufacturing, private labeling, and order-based production for a series of foreign brands in the vacuum cleaner sector.

![Original image 32](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-032.jpg)

In 1991, following the success of sales in Japan, Dyson began selling vacuum cleaners through Dyson Appliances Ltd.

![Original image 33](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-033.jpg)

In 1992, Bissell released the carpet cleaning machine Promax (which was later renamed Powerlifter due to patent issues with Hoover). Following this, Bissell introduced another product, a deep carpet cleaner called Big Green. In 1993, they released a smaller version known as Little Green. (The latest models of Big Green and Little Green are shown in the image below.)

![Original image 34](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-034.jpg)

In 1994, Mark Bissell succeeded his father, John Bissell, as the leader of the Bissell company and has held that position to this day.

![Original image 35](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-035.jpg)

In May 1994, Suzhou Vacuum Cleaner Factory, Wu County Microcomputer Factory, and Xushishi Town Asset Operating Company established Suzhou Chunhua Electrical Appliances Co., Ltd. through aprivate placement method. That year, the Vacuum Cleaner Factory obtained self-operated import and export rights, increasing vacuum cleaner production to 753,500 units and exporting 370,000 units.

In August 1994, Bian Zhuang, then Deputy Director of Suzhou Light Industry Bureau, resigned from public service to go into business and founded EUP (Suzhou EUP Electrical Appliances Co., Ltd.). Over the next decade, EUP, together with Roborock and Tineco (TEK), were listed as the top three contract manufacturers in the vacuum cleaner industry.

![Original image 36](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-036.jpg)

In October 1994, Ni Zuguang left Chunhua Electrical Appliances and borrowed $30,000 to found Kingclean Company, focusing on ODM export business for vacuum cleaners and other products centered around motors.

![Original image 37](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-037.jpg)

In 1994, Nilfisk acquired the American company Advance Machine Company and began entering the professional cleaning market in the United States.

![Original image 38](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-038.jpg)

In May 1995, Chunhua Appliances established Suzhou Chunflying Household Appliances Co., Ltd. in a joint venture with Philips of the Netherlands. Philips brought both technology and orders to the Chinese vacuum cleaner industry, making it one of the most important foreign brands introduced into the sector.

![Original image 39](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-039.jpg)

In 1995, Royal faced severe market competition. TTI planned and manufactured the Broom Vac for Royal, which was priced at $40 and sold 800,000 units.

![Original image 40](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-040.jpg)

In 1996, Ningbo Fuda Electric Appliances went public, becoming the first Initial Public Offering (IPO) in the vacuum cleaner industry.

![Original image 41](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-041.jpg)

In 1996, Li Jukun, who started in vacuum cleaner components, founded Suzhou Chunju Electrical Appliances. Leveraging a partnership with Philips, he became an important member of the second tier in the vacuum cleaner industry.

![Original image 42](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-042.jpg)

In 1996, Dongguan Grand Electric was formally established as a joint venture between the German Miele Group and the German Melitta Group with a 50:50 equity ratio, producing Miele-branded vacuum cleaners and other appliances.

![Original image 43](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-043.jpg)

In 1996, Bissell acquired the Singer brand from Ryobi Motor Products, a brand known for sewing machines and vacuum cleaners. This acquisition enabled Bissell to improve its upright vacuums and fill the gap in its lower-end product line. Over the next eight years, Bissell surpassed another century-old brand, Hoover (now part of TTI), and briefly held a 20% market share.


In 1996, Electrolux released the Trilobite robot vacuum, which was designed to resemble an ancient creature, the trilobite. This was the first robot vacuum equipped with ultrasonic sensors capable of automatically detecting obstacle positions, priced around $2000.

![Original image 44](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-044.jpg)

In 1998, Qian Dongqi founded Ecovacs Robotics Technology (Suzhou) Co., Ltd. (originally Tineco), and began independently researching and developing vacuum cleaner products.

![Original image 45](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-045.jpg)

In 1999, Eureka, the North American subsidiary of Electrolux, released a robotic vacuum at the Chicago show.

![Original image 46](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-046.jpg)

In 2000, Guangzhou Yaochuan Electronics (Shenzhen) Co., Ltd. was established to undertake the manufacturing of vacuum cleaners for brands such as Electrolux, SEB, Philips, and TTI.

![Original image 47](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-047.jpg)

In 2001, Dyson developed the robot vacuum DC06, which featured 70 sensors, 54 batteries, 3 processors, 2 large wheels, and a typical Dyson-style dustbin. It was described as "5% vacuum cleaner, 95% robot." Due to its size, weight, and significant production issues, coupled with the potential retail price of up to $6,000, DC06 was never actually released for sale.

![Original image 48](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-048.jpg)

In 2001, LG released the Roboking robot vacuum. In terms of size, it did not fall short of the Dyson DC06. This, clearly, was not a good solution.

![Original image 49](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-049.jpg)

In 2002, TTI's CEO Horst Pudwill heard market rumors that their primary customer, the vacuum cleaner manufacturer Royal Appliance (which owned Royal and Dirt Devil), was for sale. He immediately contacted Royal Appliance's CEO Mike Merriman to arrange an acquisition.

![Original image 50](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-050.jpg)

In 2002, Dyson moved its vacuum cleaner factory to Malaysia, sparking controversy within the UK.

![Original image 51](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-051.jpg)

In 2002, iRobot, which originated from military robots, released the first truehome robot vacuum Roomba, priced at $199.95, and initiated the era of intelligent robotic vacuums. The first-generation Roomba 400 series utilized a random bump cleaning mode. Upon its release in that same year, Roomba achieved tremendous success in the market.

![Original image 52](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-052.jpg)

In May 2002, German company Kärcher released the RoboCleaner, priced around $1300. Unlike others, it featured a large charging base that could automatically suck debris from the robot vacuum into a paper bag on the base, thereby saving time in cleaning the robot vacuum.

![Original image 53](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-053.jpg)

In 2002, Ningbo Fujia Real Estate was established and later became a member of the second tier in the vacuum cleaner industry by leveraging its partnership with American SharkNinja.

![Original image 54](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-054.jpg)

In 2002, Suzhou XingDesheng Motor was established and later became the largest third-party factory in the vacuum cleaner motor industry in China.

![Original image 55](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-055.jpg)

Dyson released the DC11 vacuum cleaner in 2003, and currently, even dual-bin and triple-bin designs of vacuums are inspired by Dyson.

![Original image 56](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-056.jpg)

In May 2003, Hitachi released the Cleaning Robot HCR-03.

![Original image 57](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-057.jpg)

In 2003, Samsung released the robotic vacuum VC-RP30W.

![Original image 58](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-058.jpg)

In 2004, Roborock entered the robot vacuum contract manufacturing industry.

![Original image 59](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-059.jpg)

In 2004, Electrolux released the Ergorapido wireless vacuum cleaner, which became a bestseller over the following decade, with total sales exceeding 10 million units.

![Original image 60](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-060.jpg)

In 2005, Shenzhen Yinxing Technology was founded. As one of the earlier companies to enter the robot vacuum industry, Yinxing contributed a significant number of talents to the sector.

![Original image 61](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-061.jpg)

In April 2005, Jiangsu Chunhua collaborated with Guangdong Midea Electrical Appliances Co., Ltd., and was renamed Jiangsu Midea Chunhua Electrical Appliances Co., Ltd. Xiao Jianfu, the former general manager of Chunhua, served as the general manager.

![Original image 62](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-062.jpg)

In 2005, iRobot released the Scooba floor cleaning robot.

![Original image 63](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-063.jpg)

On November 9, 2005, iRobot went public on NASDAQ following its significant success in the robot vacuum market.

![Original image 64](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-064.jpg)

In 2006, Chen Hong left Black+Decker and founded Kingclean Group. Throughout its rapid development, Kingclean became the largest manufacturer of wet-dry vacuum cleaners in China, with self-branded products accounting for the majority.

![Original image 65](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-065.jpg)

In 2007, the Euro Pro steam mop achieved significant success in the North American market. As the third-generation member of the family, Mark Rosenweig successfully transitioned the company's focus from sewing machines to cleaning products. Over the next decade, under the leadership of Mark Rosenweig (Mark R.) and his partner Mark Barrocas (Mark B.), Euro Pro grew its vacuum cleaner market share from 1% to 30%. In 2015, the company was renamed SharkNinja.

![Original image 66](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-066.jpg)

In 2007, Nilfisk acquired the professional cleaning equipment company Viper in Dongguan.

![Original image 67](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-067.jpg)

In 2007, TTI acquired Hoover's floor cleaning business for $107 million in cash from the household appliances giant Vorwerk and merged it with the original Royal and Dirt Devil businesses. As a result, TTI Floor Care became North America's largest manufacturer of cleaning appliances.

![Original image 68](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-068.jpg)

On June 11, 2007, Roborock launched its presence on Taobao, with the official store of Little Dog Appliances opening for business, becoming one of the early appliance brands toenter e-commerce platforms. Taking advantage of the rapid decade-long development of the e-commerce industry, Roborock also worked to establish its brand in the e-commerce sector.

![Original image 69](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-069.jpg)

In 2009, Ecovacs launched the Deebot series of robot vacuums, rapidly advancing in the industry and becoming a market leader.

![Original image 70](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-070.jpg)

In 2010, Guangdong Xinbao Electrical Appliances established a factory in Chuzhou, Anhui, entering the vacuum cleaner industry. In 2014, Xinbao Electrical Appliances listed on the main board, with its domestic brand being "Dongling".

![Original image 71](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-071.jpg)

In February 2010, the Neato XV-11 robotic vacuum was launched with a price of $399. This was the first robot to use a laser rangefinder capable of rotating 360 degrees to scan its surroundings and perform simultaneous localization and mapping (SLAM), enabling it to plan cleaning routes based on this information. As a result, robotic vacuums began transitioning from random cleaning to planned cleaning. Additionally, Neato could resume cleaning from where it left off if it automatically returned to charge due to low battery; once fully charged, it would continue cleaning from the point where it stopped previously.

![Original image 72](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-072.jpg)

In 2010, the German home appliance century-old company Vorwerk led Neato's $12 million Series D funding.

![Original image 73](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-073.jpg)

In 2010, the intelligent robotic vacuum company iRobot announced an agreement with Jabil, whereby the latter's Chinese factory would manufacture intelligent robot vacuums.

![Original image 74](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-074.jpg)

In 2010, Dyson sued competitor VAX (a brand under the industry giant TTI) in the UK, claiming that therival Mach Zen vacuum infringed on Dyson's dusting nozzle design patent from 15 years prior for its DC02 vacuum cleaner. Ultimately, the British court rejected Dyson's lawsuit.

![Original image 75](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-075.jpg)

In 2011, Bissell announced another of their business ventures—the carpet cleaning machine rental service under the Big Green brand—directly challenging the market leader Rug Doctor. At that time, Rug Doctor's machines were priced at $600 for sale, with a daily rental fee of $24.99 (excluding cleaner), while Bissell entered the market with a price point of $400.

With support from the retail chain Lowe's, Bissell's Big Green deep carpet cleaning machines quickly expanded to over 800 stores.

![Original image 76](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-076.jpg)

In 2011, Vorwerk released a robot vacuum with the model number VR-100, which was manufactured for them by Neato.

![Original image 77](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-077.jpg)

In 2012, Shenzhen iLife Technology began producing robot vacuums. Later, through its collaboration with Ecovacs, iLife became an important player in the robot vacuum industry.

![Original image 78](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-078.jpg)

In 2013, Oreck Corporation filed for bankruptcy, and TTI acquired Oreck, entering the commercial cleaning sector.

![Original image 79](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-079.jpg)

In 2013, TTI (Ecovacs) shut down its own vacuum cleaner manufacturing plant in China and fully shifted to contract manufacturing. Companies such as Ningbo DeChang Motor became important contract manufacturers for TTI's vacuum cleaner business, which alone was worth over 1 billion US dollars.

![Original image 80](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-080.jpg)

In June 2013, iRobot sued five companies for patent infringement of iRobot's Roomba in German courts.

The five companies were: Pardus GmbH, Emparanza y Galdos Internacional SA, Elektrogerate Solac Vertrieb GmbH, Electrodomesticos Solac SA, and Celaya. (The following image shows a comparison of their models.)

![Original image 81](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-081.jpg)

On September 1, 2014, the European Union officially implemented the first phase of the Energy-related Product (ErP) standard for vacuum cleaners, fundamentally altering the landscape of vacuum cleaners in the EU market.

![Original image 82](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-082.jpg)

In 2014, after Jiejiaobao, a subsidiary of Hong Kong Jihe Group, exited the Chinese market, Bissell and the contract manufacturing giant Flex reached an agreement to continue subcontracting vacuum cleaners at the former Jihe industrial park.

![Original image 83](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-083.jpg)

In September 2014, Dyson released the 360 Eyes, priced at $999. After failing with its robot vacuum plans over a decade ago, Dyson finally returned to the market for robotic vacuums in 2014 with thepremium robot vacuum360Eye. This robot removed more than 70 sensors from the DC06 model and instead used a camera mounted at the top to observe and analyze its surroundings in all directions. It utilized algorithms to create a map of the room and navigate accordingly, judging its current path by the changes in positions of landmarks between before-and-after images and updating and adjusting its environmental model based on this information. However, Dyson 360Eyes faced criticism for being noisy and too thick to clean under certainunder some sofas.

![Original image 84](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-084.jpg)

In 2015, Dyson sued European giants Siemens and Bosch for cheating in EU vacuum cleaner energy efficiency tests. Dyson claimed that Siemens and Bosch used sensors to send signals to the vacuum motor's electronics, enabling it to achieve an AAAA rating in dust-free test environments. However, in actual usage environments, the vacuum might only achieve a grade E or F.

In 2015, Dyson released the Dyson V6 corded handheld vacuum cleaner and began leading trends in the vacuum cleaner industry (with digital motors capable of reaching 120,000 RPM; previous motors typically operated at around 25,000 RPM, while current domestic vacuum motor factories can produce motors with speeds up to about 80,000 RPM). The entire vacuum cleaner industry saw a growth rate of 30-40% annually for corded charging products.

![Original image 85](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-085.jpg)

On May 13, 2015, Kingclean Electrical successfully listed on the Shanghai Stock Exchange. The Ni Zuguang family was once among the wealthiest in Suzhou.

![Original image 86](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-086.jpg)

On November 12, 2015, Suzhou Etta Technology (834236.OC) listed on the New Third Board.

![Original image 87](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-087.jpg)

In 2015, Panasonic launched the triangular-shaped robot vacuum Rulo.

![Original image 88](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-088.jpg)

In September 2015, iRobot introduced the latest model of their robot vacuum, Roomba 980 (priced at $899). In this new product, iRobot utilized the latest iAdapt 2.0 navigation technology. The most critical component of this technology is VSLAM and global planning functionality. At the top of the Roomba 980, unlike previous models that used LiDAR, there is a camera installed at approximately a 45-degree angle. Images captured by this camera are processed through localization algorithms to identify features within them. As a result, during operation, the robot builds an environmental map while simultaneously estimating its position in the environment. This enables precise cleaning paths and achieves high levels of cleaning coverage.

![Original image 89](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-089.jpg)

In October 2015, the North American vacuum cleaner brand Bissell, with over 140 years of history, released the Smart Cleaner robotic vacuum.

![Original image 90](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-090.jpg)

In 2016, Dyson released the V8 wireless vacuum cleaner. Relying on the powerful performance of the V8, Dyson began to gain widespread popularity globally.

![Original image 91](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-091.jpg)

In August 2016, Mi Home under Xiaomi launched the Roborockrobot vacuum, utilizing LiDAR navigation technology with global planning functionality in its algorithm. Typically, such products are priced above 4,000 yuan, whereas Roborock was sold for only 1,699 yuan. This product became a hit, with single-item sales exceeding 1 billion yuan RMB.

On December 6, 2016, due to a significant decline in the North American Eureka business, appliance giant Electrolux sold the Eureka brand for $60 million to Midea.

![Original image 94](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-094.jpg)

In September 2017, iRobot, an American robot vacuum manufacturer, filed lawsuits against eleven competitors, accusing them of infringing on six of the company's technological patents (United States Patent Numbers: 6809490, 7155308, 8474090, 8600553, 9038233, 9486924), requesting the initiation of a Section 337 investigation and seeking limited exclusion and cease and desist orders. The accused entities included three Chinese companies and eight foreign enterprises: Shenzhen iLife Technology Co., Ltd., Suzhou Lebao Electrical Appliances Co., Ltd., Shenzhen Yinxing Intelligent Technology Co., Ltd., Bissell of the United States, Hoover (a brand under TTI), Royal Appliance (also a TTI brand), bObsweep of both the United States and Canada, Black & Decker of the United States, Taiwan Songteng Industrial, and Micro-Star International (MSI). Micro-Star subsequently reached a settlement with iRobot and exited the robot vacuum market.

![Original image 95](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-095.jpg)

In September 2017, the well-known small appliance brand SharkNinja released its first robot vacuum, ION Robot, priced at $299. The market generally believed that iRobot would face significant pressure as a result, leading to a decline in iRobot's stock price by more than 15%.

![Original image 96](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-096.jpg)

On August 18, 2017, CDH Investment announced the acquisition of SharkNinja, a leading brand in the North American market. The acquisition reportedly involved a figure in the hundreds of millions or even billions of dollars. Behind this deal is an implicit shadow of listed company Joyoung, which has also led to a shift in the industry's OEM landscape.

![Original image 97](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-097.jpg)

On September 1, 2017, the second phase of the EU Energy-related Product (ErP II) standard came into full effect, requiring the nominal power to be no more than 900W, with actual power usage generally not exceeding 700W.

![Original image 98](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-098.jpg)

On September 15, 2017, Zhongqian Corporation announced the termination of its plan to acquire shares in Guangdong Baole Robot Co., Ltd.

![Original image 99](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-099.jpg)

On September 22, 2017, the German household appliance brand Vorwerk (which achieved revenues of $3.6 billion in 2016) announced the acquisition of the remaining shares of Neato, a company ranked second in the robotic vacuum market. As a result, Neato became a wholly-owned subsidiary of Vorwerk.

![Original image 100](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-100.jpg)

On December 22, 2017, Ecovacs submitted its prospectus, with founder Qian Dongqí and his son Qian Cheng as the largest shareholders.

![Original image 101](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-101.jpg)

By the end of 2017, Songteng Real Estate utilized Taiwan Industrial Research Institute's VSLAM patent to file a lawsuit against iRobot with the China Intellectual Property Office. Ultimately, Songteng Real Estate and iRobot reached a settlement agreement, mutually agreeing not to sue each other, and allowing Songteng Real Estate to enter the U.S. market.

![Original image 102](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-102.jpg)

On March 20, 2018, IPO approval was granted to Ecovacs Robotics. According to Ecovacs' prospectus, sales for the first half of 2017 reached RMB 1.966 billion. In 2016, Shenzhen iLife Technology Co., Ltd. manufactured robots for Ecovacs amounting to RMB 490 million.

In March 2018, Roborock Technology launched another robot vacuum priced at 999 yuan, aiming to capture the low-end random navigation market. Will this product be able to replicate past success?

In 2018, Airtek (Yaoke Electric), which had shut down, saw its clients subsequently shift to other contract manufacturers in the industry.

In August 2018, Bissell acquired the Sanitaire brand from Electrolux and entered the commercial cleaning market.

![Original image 107](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-107.jpg)

On November 8, 2018, after continuous appeals from Dyson Company, the EU energy efficiency standard will be repealed.

![Original image 108](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-108.jpg)

From 2017 to 2018, the Spanish company Cecotec (under the Conga brand) capitalized on the rise of e-commerce and saw its robot vacuum shipments reach hundreds of thousands of units in a short period. This also reflects the growing prominence of the global e-commerce industry during this time.

![Original image 109](/images/insights/a-century-of-cleaning-appliances-image-109.jpg)

In March 2019, Dyson released the latest V11 wireless vacuum cleaner, which features intelligent sensors marking the entry of the vacuum cleaner industry into the smart era. Additionally, the V11's configuration with a 21700 battery also leads the industry towards larger capacity batteries.

In March 2019, TTI launched the strategic One Power series, re-establishing a universal battery platform for household appliances. If this attempt is successful, it will provide a standardized model for the wireless transformation of the entire small appliance industry.
