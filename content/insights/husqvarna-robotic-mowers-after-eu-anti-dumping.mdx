Robotic mowers moved onto the promotional front line around Prime Day in both the United States and Europe. Chinese brands used wire-free navigation, vision, RTK, LiDAR and all-wheel drive to compete at prices around $1,000 to $2,000, and sometimes below that range.

![Robotic mower price competition](/images/blog/husqvarna-robotic-mowers-after-eu-anti-dumping-image-002.webp)

Husqvarna has discounted products as well. In May 2026, the Automower 410iQ appeared on Amazon at $1,799.99, down from $2,599.99, with blades, a charging station and an EPOS reference station included. But that looked more like a seasonal promotion on one premium model than a broad reset of the brand's pricing system.

The pressure is not on one product. It is on the price logic Husqvarna has built around Automower over many years.

Husqvarna has dealers, installers, service capability and the trust of professional users. In the past, many buyers chose a low-friction brand before comparing every specification. Now the specification table sits in the center of the buying decision. When products with similar headline functions sell for roughly half the price of established European models, a €2,000 to €3,000 mower needs a clearer explanation.

Husqvarna is responding with a wider boundary-wire-free range and AI Vision. It is also bringing lower-priced models into smaller gardens. Yet aggressive online discounting creates a second problem for a traditional dealer-led company: it can weaken dealer margins, installation economics and the value attached to long-term service.

Chinese companies are bringing the robot-vacuum playbook onto the lawn: faster launches, more visible specifications, review-led demand and platform promotions. Buyers who once started with a dealer increasingly start with Amazon, YouTube and a Prime Day alert.

## Anti-dumping changes the route, not the destination

In November 2025, the European Commission opened an anti-dumping proceeding concerning robotic lawn mowers from China. The complaint was lodged by Husqvarna Manufacturing CZ. In January 2026, the EU made those imports subject to registration, preserving the possibility of collecting duties retroactively.

![How anti-dumping may redirect the supply chain](/images/blog/husqvarna-robotic-mowers-after-eu-anti-dumping-image-003.webp)

The proceeding can raise the cost and uncertainty of direct exports from China. Importers and dealers must reconsider inventory and pricing. But Chinese manufacturers can also relocate assembly, reconfigure sourcing and use Southeast Asian production. If European demand remains attractive, companies will search for another route to market.

This is why anti-dumping is a defensive card rather than a complete answer. It can buy time. It cannot restore an old price reference once consumers have seen capable wire-free machines at much lower prices.

## Husqvarna's strongest card is still reliability

Husqvarna can still sell a simpler ownership experience: dependable operation, a service network, spare parts and years of maintenance. Chinese brands still need stronger local after-sales teams, parts warehouses and dealer relationships. If they combine lower prices with low-friction ownership, Husqvarna's premium will be recalculated.

![Glen Instone and Husqvarna's response](/images/blog/husqvarna-robotic-mowers-after-eu-anti-dumping-image-004.webp)

Glen Instone became Husqvarna Group CEO in August 2025 after leading the Forest & Garden Division. The appointment put an executive from the business closest to robotic mower competition at the top of the group. Husqvarna's 2025 net sales were SEK 46.6 billion, so one product category will not break the company. But the pressure reveals a strategic contradiction.

Husqvarna is using European trade defense against Chinese price pressure. At the same time, faster iteration, lower cost and a more responsive supply chain may require the company to work more closely with Chinese engineering and manufacturing capabilities.

The bigger question is therefore not whether Husqvarna can survive a mower price war. It is whether it can use the capabilities of China to compete more effectively with Chinese brands.
