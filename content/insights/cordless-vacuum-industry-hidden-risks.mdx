Reasons for fires in robot vacuums include:

1. Unqualified battery cells. Some unscrupulous manufacturers use recycled or cement cells to cut costs.
2. Deficiencies in circuit board design, lacking proper protection against overcharging and overdischarging. These defects can lead to issues during charging.
3. The absence of industry-mandated standards for robot vacuums, leading to inherent safety problems in many designs.
4. A large number of small workshops entering the market, producing low-quality products.

From this, it is clear that while social media has contributed to the rapid rise of balance scooters, their poor quality has also led to a swift decline.

Currently, cordless vacuum cleaners are experiencing growth within the industry, with many manufacturers who have not previously produced vacuums now entering the market.

1. These new entrants lack awareness of the risks associated with lithium-ion batteries, leading to inadequate safety design considerations and potential inherent safety issues.
2. Some companies are using recycled cells in an effort to reduce costs and secure orders.
3. Due to the relatively recent development of cordless vacuum cleaners over the past two years, most small and medium-sized enterprises do not have dedicated electronic engineers. The battery management systems (BMS) for these batteries often rely on third-party solution providers, leaving them without the ability to verify or resolve issues.
4. As cordless vacuum cleaners become more prevalent, many small packagers are emerging, focusing solely on assembly rather than quality control at the source. This means that defective packs cannot be identified and resolved.

I have outlined a number of potential industry issues, but I remain optimistic about the development of cordless vacuum cleaners. However, I hope that no manufacturers compromise on battery quality to secure orders, as substandard batteries could lead to serious consequences such as explosions, which would severely impact the entire vacuum cleaner industry.
