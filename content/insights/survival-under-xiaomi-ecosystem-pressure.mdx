"Dug a hole, Xiaomi has launched another new product at such a low price; is there any room left for survival in this industry?"

As one of the struggling manufacturers, I hear information related to Xiaomi every day. Companies that have already entered industries through Xiaomi's ecosystem are closely watching the movements within the Xiaomi sphere. Those companies that haven't yet ventured into Xiaomi's ecosystem live in constant fear, worrying about when Xiaomi might enter and snatch away their market share.

While everyone is familiar with Xiaomi, there seems to be some confusion among industry partners regarding what exactly constitutes a company from Xiaomi's ecosystem. Ordinary consumers are even more clueless.

"Using investment as a means, finding the best teams, leveraging Xiaomi’s platform and resources to help enterprises create top-notch products, and quickly positioning them in the internet market."

In simpler terms, Xiaomi invests in talented individuals across various industries, then supports these invested companies with its own scale for product design, quality control, and sales. This allows these companies to build significant momentum before their products hit the market, ensuring a rapid rise to prominence.

Most of the products from Xiaomi's ecosystem are sold on "Xiaomi Youpin," while the best among them carry the "Mi Home" brand and are sold directly on Xiaomi’s official website.

Currently, Xiaomi's ecosystem has achieved remarkable results, sweeping through industries with overwhelming force. Traditional enterprises have all been left in awe. There are already many articles online detailing this; we won't delve into it further here.

For those of us working in industries that have been invaded by Xiaomi's ecosystem, the pressing concern is how to survive under Xiaomi’s iron heel. It would be even better if we could reclaim some of our market share.

**Is there any possibility for this?**

First, let's examine the diagram above, which highlights the advantages of Xiaomi's ecosystem chain. It is evident that compared to traditional manufacturing companies, these advantages are substantial. We will analyze each aspect one by one to see if there are any opportunities.


### 1. Quality Standards

Having interacted with many friends from Xiaomi, it is clear that the quality standards for enterprises in Xiaomi’s ecosystem are extremely stringent—among the best in the industry. This strictness can increase production complexity and costs. However, if a product maintains a large sales volume, high-quality standards will become a positive factor, enhancing its reputation and sales through a virtuous cycle. Conversely, insufficient sales volumes may render these stringent requirements burdensome for suppliers, leading to increased manufacturing costs and reduced supplier cooperation.

For instance, the failure of Rong Yonghao's Huami smartphone was due to excessive attention to detail, causing Foxconn’s support to wane over time, ultimately resulting in delayed product launches and current challenges.

Previously, successful enterprises within Xiaomi’s ecosystem deliberately emphasized their stringent quality control during marketing campaigns. This gave some companies a false sense that perfecting quality control alone would guarantee success. However, a successful product requires more than just high-quality standards; it also needs good design, functionality, and effective marketing strategies. Moreover, certain quality requirements can be contradictory, making the essence of product development an ongoing process of compromise.

Xiaomi’s products generally exceed market average quality levels, with most companies finding it difficult to match these standards. In this area, the goal should be to approach Xiaomi's quality standards as closely as possible. While Xiaomi’s commitment to quality control is undeniable, there can sometimes be a trap in over-engineering products. If the market standard is 70 points, Xiaomi might aim for 99 points, which would be costly and yield little additional benefit. Lowering the quality standard to 85 could significantly reduce production costs without much noticeable difference to ordinary consumers.


### 2. Product Design

In summary, while the stringent quality standards of Xiaomi’s ecosystem are a significant advantage, they also come with challenges. Companies must balance these high standards with practical considerations and find ways to optimize their product development processes.

![Original image 2](/images/insights/survival-under-xiaomi-ecosystem-pressure-image-002.jpg)

The design standards of the ID design in Xiaomi's ecosystem are unparalleled in traditional manufacturing industries. The minimalist and cold modern style is also a personal favorite.

Xiaomi has very strict requirements for the design of its ecosystem products, with veto power over them. Without Xiaomi's assistance, many companies would struggle to produce products at this level.

Minimalism is a hallmark of Xiaomi’s ecosystem (similar to Muji), which could also be seen as a potential drawback. Minimalism is just one category among various design styles. A fixed design style can limit the target consumer base and potentially the market ceiling. For a product, several models might adopt a minimalist style as a series; however, should minimalism become the standard direction for the company?

Another issue: If there are 3-5 companies producing the same product within Xiaomi's ecosystem, all with similar minimalist designs, how will consumers make their choices? Of course, from a consumer’s perspective, it doesn’t matter which brand they choose since all are backed by Xiaomi. However, for the ecosystem companies themselves, this could be a matter of life and death.

For traditional enterprises, they can release products with a similar minimalist style to Xiaomi's while keeping prices competitive. This would confuse consumers about product positioning and break Xiaomi’s monopoly on the minimalist design market. Currently, Xiaomi's ID design far exceeds the market average, but if traditional enterprises can approach Xiaomi in terms of design, it will significantly reduce one of Xiaomi's key selling points.

**3. Channels.**

 Xiaomi's sales channels are at the core of its business model. The crowdfunding on Xiaomi's official platform and website allows ecosystem companies to secure significant pre-sale orders from the outset when their products launch, while subsequent online sales can generate substantial revenue for these products. It can be said that many ecosystem companies rely heavily on Xiaomi’s channels for survival.

A pressing issue is that user growth in mobile internet has plateaued, entering a mature market phase. As Xiaomi continues to invest in new ecosystem companies, the competition for channel resources among these companies becomes increasingly fierce. Failing to secure core channel resources from Xiaomi could be a matter of life and death for an ecosystem company.

Given Xiaomi's positioning as a cost-effective brand, its channel margins are relatively low at around 15%. This means that if a company tries to sell through other channels without sufficient margin to compensate platforms and distributors, it will struggle to make sales. Typically, the profit margin for self-branded products in retail is between 40-50%, so with only a 15% margin, there are no resources left for product development.

Due to these low margins, Xiaomi cannot offer reasonable profits to channel partners, making their collaboration merely a one-time transaction. Essentially, it's a business-to-business relationship where channelers/agents do not consider brand maintenance or other issues. Xiaomi’s loss in the smartphone market to Oppo and Vivo underscores that just having a loud voice is insufficient; partners must also have a strong voice.

By providing reasonable profits to channel partners, they can help amplify the brand’s reach further. Additionally, offering partners adequate resources such as a product with similar configuration but lower price fortraffic acquisition (attracting customers) purposes, followed by recommending higher-margin products once the customer base is established, would be beneficial.

Building deep relationships with partners to achieve mutual defense and growth.

**4. Supply Chain.**

For traditional manufacturing companies, Xiaomi's supply chain is out of reach; even major industry players struggle to directly collaborate with Xiaomi due to significant size disparities. Xiaomi’s annual sales exceed billions of dollars, whereas many leading traditional manufacturers have annual revenues in the tens of billions, creating a severe imbalance.

Ecosystem companies leverage Xiaomi’s supply chain for high-quality products and competitive pricing, enabling them to develop superior products. They can also secure the best prices and technical support from contract manufacturers and downstream suppliers, which is invaluable for startups.

However, how do the relationships among supply chain partners, ecosystem companies, and Xiaomi work?

Supply Chain Companies → Ecosystem Companies → Xiaomi Corporation.

Xiaomi provides ID design and sales channels, while supply chain companies are responsible for product realization, engineering implementation, mass production, and after-sales service. The primary role of ecosystem companies is early-stage product definition and project management (with significant involvement from Xiaomi). Given this setup, why doesn’t Xiaomi directly collaborate with supply chain partners (contract manufacturers) to potentially reduce costs by 8-10%, pushing the cost-effectiveness even further?

Huawei’s selection process follows a similar model, directly partnering with industry leaders.

Of course, Xiaomi may not directly engage with the supply chain, but if it were to take this step, what would happen to the ecosystem companies?

**5. Brand**

Currently, most ecosystem companies under Xiaomi typically start selling their products on Mi Home with their own brands. If the product performs well, it may be selected by Xiaomi to carry the Mijia brand. Once this happens, sales can increase tenfold compared to before. The majority of ecosystem companies aim to secure the Mijia brand for increased sales and then promote another brand through other channels to generate profits.

Similar to the Mijia brand and their own "Stones" brand robot vacuums, as well as the headphones from Wanmo and Xiaomi.

One drawback of this model is balancing product positioning and pricing between one's own brand and the Mijia brand. For instance, Wanmo’s own-brand headphones sold on Xiaomi.com are more expensive by a third compared to similar products under different brands, leading some to criticize Wanmo for capitalizing on the brand.

If an ecosystem company’s own-brand products outperform or match Xiaomi’s counterparts in other channels, it could create new competition for Xiaomi. This might not be the desired outcome.

Moreover, if an ecosystem company’s own-brand product achieves significant success (higher gross margins and stronger performance), it is possible that a new brand within the ecosystem will emerge to compete with lower-margin products.

For example, the first-generation "Mijia" robot vacuum cleaner from Stones sold for 1,699 yuan with a gross margin of about 16%. The Stones' own-brand all-in-one cleaning robot was priced at 2,799 yuan on other platforms and had a gross margin of around 45%. In this scenario, could the ecosystem see another low-margin all-in-one cleaning robot competing against Stones?

**6. Market Segments**

It is well-known that Xiaomi’s choice of market segments follows the principle of "ant markets," where there are no major brands or original leaders with extremely high-priced products. This allows ecosystem companies to enter the market by adopting similar configurations as top brands but offering unparalleled value for money.

For an ecosystem company to successfully enter and dominate a market, several conditions must be met:

1. The market is relatively chaotic, lacking prominent brand names. Xiaomi-backed ecosystem companies can easily capture market share. For example, Zimi in the power bank market.
2. Leading brands have high prices. When Stones entered the robot vacuum cleaner market, iRobot and Ecovacs’ products were priced between 3,000 to 4,000 yuan, while Stones sold for just 1,699 yuan, instantly creating a sensation.

However, if traditional brands dominate the market with limited price premiums, Xiaomi ecosystem companies will find it difficult to create a buzz. If the initial hit product strategy fails, subsequent efforts may be limited. This might explain why many of Xiaomi’s star ecosystem companies emerged in earlier years, but few have made significant strides recently.

**7. Pricing**

![Original image 5](/images/insights/survival-under-xiaomi-ecosystem-pressure-image-005.jpg)

The audience/followers of Xiaomi are predominantly concentrated among 15-30 year-old males who are very particular about product performance and value for money. If the pricing of Xiaomi products is not perceived as "low enough," it can lead to dissatisfaction from this segment of consumers. Early on, when Xiaomi entered certain markets, they often offered "better performance at half the price." However, if subsequent products fail to meet these expectations, some fans may leave negative reviews.

In a mature market, such opportunities are rare and fleeting—perhaps only lasting for a few months. Treating such exceptions as norms can lead to unrealistic consumer expectations.

Similarly, users who prioritize value for money have no brand loyalty. For instance, when Xiaomi's Yi Camera faced competition from 360, which offered similar products at lower prices, it had to invest in three more camera companies just to regain market share.

**8. Marketing and Promotion**

Generally, Xiaomi ecosystem companies invest very little in marketing (due to profit margin concerns), relying heavily on the channels provided by the Xiaomi brand itself. Reduced marketing efforts can decrease awareness of these brands outside the Xiaomi ecosystem, thereby reducing sales from external sources.

Enterprises could leverage other social media platforms such as Xiaohongshu and Douyin for promotion. This not only helps in building a brand but also drives sales for channel partners, fostering deeper partnerships.

Dyson, a major player in the vacuum cleaner industry, invests hundreds of millions annually in marketing efforts in China, which has helped it maintain its top position in the market. Xiaomi's value-for-money approach has had no significant impact on Dyson’s market share.

**9. After-sales Service**

After-sales service is another issue for Xiaomi ecosystem companies. Many consumers report receiving cold and automated responses from customer support online. The root cause may be a lack of sufficient resources dedicated to after-sales services.

**10. Product Development**

Most Xiaomi ecosystem enterprises rely on contract manufacturers and supply chains for their R&D capabilities, making it difficult to develop cutting-edge products. As a result, the core focus of most Xiaomi ecosystem products is on ID design (minimalist style), quality control, and more expensive materials—essentially focusing on value for money.

Previously, "getting two dollars' worth of product for one dollar" was the approach; now, Xiaomi offers "getting twenty dollars' worth of product for ten dollars." This strategy has been highly effective but poses a low barrier to entry. If all core technologies come from partners, it becomes easy for external competitors and even other companies within the Xiaomi ecosystem to replicate these functionalities.

Although there are many notable enterprises like Yunmi, Zimi, and STONE in the Xiaomi ecosystem, as well as numerous underperforming ones whose names we can no longer recall, each company has its unique characteristics. It is not accurate to generalize all of them with a single label such as "Xiaomi ecosystem companies."

In conclusion, as Lei Jun once said, "Life and death are but trivial matters; challenge any unfairness."
